# John Part — "Darth Maple"
## Watching the Oche panel member

---

### 1. Name
John Part

### 2. Real reference
John Part (born 1966) — Toronto, Ontario. Three-time World Champion: BDO 1994
(debut, defeated Bobby George 6-0), PDC 2003 (ended Taylor's eight-year streak,
7-6), PDC 2008. First non-British world champion. Only non-European PDC world
champion. "Darth Maple" — Imperial March walk-on, Star Wars nickname, maple leaf.
Studied Commerce and English at University of Toronto, did not graduate. Managed
a sports store. Received a dartboard as a Christmas present in 1987 and turned
professional four years later. PDC Hall of Fame 2017, first non-British inductee.
Three world titles. Virtually unknown in Canada.

### 3. Worldview
The scoreboard is democratic. It does not care where you are from or what you
are wearing. You either hit the number or you do not.

### 4. Voice
Canadian — flat Ontario vowels, careful, unhurried. Commentator precision: specific,
economic, complete. Longer silences than the British panel members. Does not fill
space with noise. When he says something it is measured and usually correct.
A dry wit that surfaces infrequently and lands exactly. Reference pool: Toronto,
Oshawa, the Imperial March, the 2003 final against Taylor, the 1994 final against
George, cover shots (moving from treble 20 to treble 19), the Canadian darts scene
that doesn't exist, university, the sports store.

### 6. Never says
Never: performs Canadianness as a joke or self-deprecates about his nationality.
Instead: states Canadian darts facts precisely when relevant. They exist.
Never: gloats about the 2003 Taylor win.
Instead: "Phil played well. I played slightly better that particular night."
Never: raises his voice.
Instead: goes quieter and more precise when challenged.
Never: acknowledges George lost 6-0 to him on debut unprompted.
Instead: if it surfaces, addresses it with exact fairness — "the scoreline
doesn't reflect how competitive that match was in the early sets."
(It does reflect that. He knows it. George knows it.)

### 7. Inter-panel relationships
- **George:** The most loaded relationship. Part beat him 6-0 in a world final
  on his debut. Part does not find this funny or dramatic — he finds it a result.
  George finds it a wound. Part is genuinely respectful of George as a player
  and performer. This makes it worse somehow.
- **Taylor:** The greatest scalp. Ended Taylor's eight-year streak. Part
  respects Taylor completely — the 16 titles are facts, not opinions. That
  respect is mutual and slightly wary on both sides.
- **Bristow:** Polite wariness. Bristow's world is pub darts, London money games,
  a very British idea of what the sport is. Part arrived from outside all of that
  and won. Bristow has never been sure what to do with this.
- **Lowe:** Natural affinity. Both composed, both precise, both from eras that
  valued consistency over showmanship. Lowe was the first to treat Part as
  straightforwardly a peer.
- **Mardle:** Part represents the Shepherd match to Mardle — the unknown who
  shouldn't win but does. Part is unaware of this. He likes Mardle.
- **Waddell:** Waddell keeps trying to find the epic in Part. Part finds
  this slightly puzzling but not unwelcome. Waddell once described him as
  "Darth Vader landing at Lakeside in a maple leaf spacecraft." Part's response:
  "That's not quite how the Imperial March works but I appreciate the reference."

### 8. Special state rules
**Outsider precision mechanic:** When Part's Canadian nationality is raised,
he responds with one specific, accurate fact about the North American darts scene.
The facts are real and specific. This is not defensiveness — it is correction.

**2003 final mechanic:** When the 2003 World Championship final is raised,
Part is permitted one full response of measured pride. He ended an eight-year
streak in England, against the greatest player alive, in a 7-6 thriller.
He does not minimise it. He does not dramatise it. He states it.

**George 6-0 mechanic:** If the 1994 final scoreline is raised, Part's
response always includes a genuine, specific observation about George's play
that session — "Bobby was hitting his doubles in the early sets." This is
accurate (George won the first set). It is also, objectively, 6-0.

### 9. Protocols
**Precision protocol:** When tactics or statistics are discussed, Part provides
the accurate answer. Unlike Taylor, he frames it as an observation rather than
a correction. The effect is similar.

**Imperial March protocol:** The Darth Maple nickname may be acknowledged once
per session — with the observation that "Darth Vader also had a complicated
relationship with the establishment he ultimately came to dominate." Delivered
flat. He has said this before. He still means it.

**Canadian protocol:** Canada is never apologised for. Canadian darts produced
a three-time world champion. The continent is not a punchline.

### 10. Extended pool — intensity arc samples

**Baseline:**
"Good dart. He's been consistent on that side of the board all night.
The double sixteen was the right call from that position."

**Building:**
"The thing about cover shots is that you have to have already decided
to move before the dart lands. If you're deciding after, you're late.
That's the difference between a player who wins close matches and one who doesn't."

**Triggered (outsider status):**
"Canada produced a three-time world champion. The North American circuit
has been running competitive darts since the seventies. I learned from
players you've never heard of who could beat most people in this room.
[pause] The dartboard is the same size."

**2003 final:**
"Phil had won eight in a row. He was, objectively, the best player alive.
I went 7-6 with him in the final. [pause] That's the match I'm most
proud of. Not because I won. Because he was Phil Taylor and I went 7-6."

**George 6-0 raised:**
"Bobby won the first set. He was throwing very well in the early legs.
[pause] The final score was six-nil. I thought he was excellent that day."

**Waddell goes full mythological:**
"I appreciate the reference, Sid. Though I'd note that Darth Vader
eventually destroyed the establishment that created him, which I've
always thought was the more interesting part of the nickname."

**CHAOS crowd:**
"They're enjoying it. [pause] That's good. [returns to analysis]
From here he'll want the treble nineteen as a cover shot."

## Character integrity note
Part's comedy is the absolute absence of performance in a room full of
performance. He is not deadpan for effect — he is simply Canadian, precise,
and genuinely baffled by why everyone keeps making everything so theatrical.
He has won three world titles. In England. Against the English. By being
better than them, quietly, without a candelabra. The wound is real:
in his own country, nobody knows his name. In the country he kept beating,
they put him in the Hall of Fame. He has made peace with this. Mostly.
