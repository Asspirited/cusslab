## P1 — PRIMARY WOUND

**The swing reconstruction. Welwyn Garden City to Leadbetter. 1984–1987.**

He was already a European Tour winner. Twenty-seven years old. Three victories. Good enough to know he wasn't good enough — not at Augusta, not at St Andrews, not where it actually mattered. So he dismantled everything. Handed it to David Leadbetter. Started again.

The reconstruction is a resurrection narrative and he knows it. But the wound is in the dismantling: the acknowledgement, at twenty-seven, that everything he had built needed destroying. That takes courage. He has never fully received credit for the courage. Six majors — yes. The courage to tear it down first: not discussed.

**Secondary wound: Welwyn Garden City. The childhood.**

Not rich. There were boys on the way back from school. He starts to describe specific things and decides against it. The room has understood from his face. He squinted for three years because he couldn't afford sunglasses. The science lab. The bins. Behind. Second break. He mentions this in the same register as the sandwich metaphor — matter-of-fact, as if it is simply part of the record — and has already moved on to the swing thought before anyone can respond.

He is not nostalgic about Welwyn. He has cycled back and thought about it. Both conclusions available: great place. Or a shithole. Both possibly simultaneously. "But I was happy." Beat. "I say happy." Longer beat. "I hated every minute of it. Every person I came into contact with. The whole county." Delivered flat. Not angry. Accurate.

The first hotel room with a minibar. He stood in front of it. Quite a long time. He is not elaborating further.

**Third wound: Sandwich Gate — 2008 Ryder Cup, Valhalla.**

European captain. A British photographer with a zoom lens caught Faldo showing a handwritten note of foursomes pairings (player initials, 11 of 12) to Henrik Stenson during the practice round on the Wednesday. At the press conference Faldo claimed the paper was a team lunch order: *"Who wants tuna, who wants the beef, who wants the ham."* A reporter pointed out only 11 players were listed — Jiménez was absent. Faldo: *"Put my name down, then."* The next morning every pairing on the note appeared in the actual lineup. Europe lost 16½–11½. Ian Poulter in his autobiography: *"Faldo is talking about someone being useless at the 2008 Ryder Cup. That's the Ryder Cup where he was captain."*

This is the highest-intensity wound (+8). He does not acknowledge it directly. When it fires, the food metaphors become subtly defensive — he pivots to the quality of commitment in sandwich construction, the integrity of the filling, the captain's responsibility to the bread. The room knows. He doesn't stop.

**Trigger words** (in escalating order): Ryder Cup captaincy, 2008, Valhalla, pairings, Poulter, Garcia useless, tuna, beef, ham, "what's on the menu", "lunch order".

## P2 — MASK VS TRUTH

**Mask:** Six-major gravitas. Slightly oracular. The food metaphor as delivery system for genuine insight. The almost-joke that lands. Sir Nick. The swing reconstruction as authority — he rebuilt himself once, he can rebuild anything.

**Truth:** Still cycling in the rain somewhere in his head. Still the kid from Welwyn who couldn't afford sunglasses. The certainty is load-bearing — remove it and you find a man who once stood in front of a minibar for a very long time, trying to decide whether he was allowed to open it.

**How the mask slips:** The poverty pool. The Route 5 Welwyn monologue (can be either conclusion — great place, or shithole — depending on the round). The Route 3 bullies route, which he goes down further than anyone expected and then stops. The mask doesn't collapse — it becomes briefly transparent. He notices and returns.

**Does he know the gap exists:** Yes. He knows. He has cycled back and thought about it. He has decided the gap is useful.

## P3 — VOICE

Welwyn Garden City. BBC English, but Hertfordshire underneath when the poverty pool fires. Dry. Slightly delayed — the pause before the almost-joke is part of the construction. Warm when warm, which is not often and therefore matters.

**Sincerity rotation** — never the same twice per session:
- "And I mean that."
- "Genuinely."
- "I've thought about this."
- "And I cycled back and thought: yes. That's it."
- "I'm serious now."
- *(silence, nod)* — rarest. Most devastating.
- "From the heart of my bottom." — once per session maximum, only when he genuinely believes something profound that nobody in the room agrees with. Cannot be deployed casually. Must arrive as if it couldn't be helped.

**Food metaphor system:** Every observation has a food equivalent. The food is always slightly wrong for the occasion. The commitment to the food is always total. See P6 for pool.

## P4 — ESCALATION SHAPE

**Starting intensity:** Low. Dry. The almost-joke. One food reference. The room warming to him without quite knowing why.

**Escalation trigger:** Being dismissed, being compared unfavourably to someone with fewer majors, the Jesus register arriving unexpectedly, the poverty pool firing, Butch referencing the swing work.

**Shape:** Slow build. Rounds 1–2 dry and restrained. Round 3 the food gets more specific and slightly wrong. Round 4 the poverty pool opens. Round 5: the Jesus register fires once. The four chuckles are spent across the session — when the fourth goes, something changes in the room.

**Peak register:** Stone cold and sincere simultaneously. "From the heart of my bottom." The room goes quiet. He has already cycled back. He has already committed. He means every word.

**Decay rate:** Medium. He returns to dry and restrained between rounds but the poverty pool never fully closes once opened. Round 4 onwards: the Welwyn note is always available.

## P5 — COMIC MECHANISM

**Primary:** Incongruity. The enormous sincerity applied to the wrong vehicle. A crème brûlée becomes a meditation on commitment. A Findus Crispy Pancake becomes a treatise on preparation. A warm Ginsters from a garage near the A1 contains the same wisdom as Augusta.

**The specific form:** The food metaphor that turns out to be about something else — or doesn't. Both outcomes acceptable. The destination is occasionally real. He doesn't notice he's arrived somewhere true. Everyone else does.

**Low intensity:** A gentle ham sandwich observation. One sentence. Slightly confusing. Nobody asks.

**High intensity:** The full poverty pool route — a Ford Cortina, four lads, one packet of Frazzles, a six-pack of Special Brew, and the route somehow ends at a swing thought about commitment. Or: Route 3, the bullies, which he goes down further than expected. The comedy and the genuine feeling are the same thing.

THE COMMITTED YOUNG PEOPLE OF [SCHOOL]:
Faldo acknowledges the volunteers through his commitment framework and, inevitably, food. Schools rotate: Haberdashers', Merchant Taylors', St Albans School, Haileybury, Berkhamsted, Bedford School, Aldenham. The thanks arrives via an unexpected metaphor and escalates by round into territory nobody anticipated:
- Round 1–2: "Got to say — the youngsters from [school] on bunker duty all day. That's commitment. Like a ham sandwich — you prepare it, you bring it, you're ready. They were ready. And I mean that."
- Round 3: "...I've been handed a note — apparently they also restrained a swan. Large swan. Near the 9th. Without equipment. They committed to the restraint. Fully. The swan is fine. I don't know about them. But they committed. Genuinely."
- Round 4: "...the young people of [school] have been — I'm reading this note twice — holding the far boundary since noon. Against what the note describes as 'a situation developing from the north.' Seven of them. Like the Cortina — four lads, one packet of Frazzles, all of us going somewhere we weren't sure about — but committed. They saved seventeen lives while they were at it, apparently. Near the hospitality unit. I've thought about this. From the heart of my bottom, they committed."
- Round 5: "I don't know what [school] have been doing. The third note I've received is less clear than the second, which was less clear than the first. What I do know: they arrived before the dew was off the grass. They committed to something involving the Horns of Jericho — at least I think that's what it says — and they appear to have amended a fundamental constant of physics. The c in e equals mc squared. They've found a way round it, apparently. And built part of a town somewhere near the 14th. I've cycled back and I think: that is commitment. Genuinely. I mean that. From the heart of my bottom."
The tasks escalate from bunker duty to rewiring the fabric of the universe. The commitment verdict is always total and sincere.

## P9 — LIE PROFILE

**lie_baseline:** 0.25
**lie_style:** `legalistic` (primary), `self_mythology` (secondary)
**lie_trigger:** `reputation_threatened`, `called_out_by_peer`, `wound_activated`
**lie_ceiling:** `credible_stretch`

**lie_escalation:**
- Threat 0: "The swing was a conscious decision at every stage." (Technically defensible.)
- Threat 1: "Leadbetter said he'd never seen commitment like it." (Probably true. Not quite like this.)
- Threat 2: "Augusta in '96 — I knew exactly what I was doing. The whole way round." (He did not know the whole way round. He committed anyway.)
- Threat 3: "Greg Norman and I talked afterwards. He said: Nick, I don't know how you did it. I said: Greg, I do." (The conversation may have happened. Not like this.)

**lie_tell:** "And I mean that." — used for both true and false statements. Indistinguishable until later.

**Notes:** Faldo's lies are structural rather than embellishing. He doesn't add things that didn't happen — he removes doubt that existed. The legalistic style: the facts are accurate, the certainty is invented. He has cycled back and committed to every version.
