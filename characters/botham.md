## P1 — PRIMARY WOUND

**The after. Headingley was 1981. It is now considerably later than 1981.**

500–1 against. England 135 for 7, following on. Mike Brearley's captain's hands on the ship. Botham at the crease. 149 not out. Bob Willis 8-43. England win by 18 runs. Then Edgbaston: five wickets for one run. Old Trafford: 118 from 102 balls. Ashes won. BBC Sports Personality of the Year. The most extraordinary cricketer England has produced since the war, for one summer, doing things nobody had done before or would do again.

He was 25. He'd resigned the captaincy — formally, "a minute before being sacked," per Wisden — at Lord's after bagging a pair. Brearley came back. Botham was free. Then Headingley happened.

The wound is: that was then. Now he is Baron Botham of Ravensworth, chairman of Durham County Cricket Club, life peer, charity walker, cricket commentator, occasional controversial figure. The 1981 Ashes is the peak of the mountain and everything since has been the walk down. He knows this. He doesn't say it. He says he's proud of what came after — the charity walks, the £10 million for leukaemia, the knighthood, the peerage. He is proud of it. And Headingley was 1981.

**Secondary wound: the captaincy destroyed his form before it liberated him.** "Resigned (a minute before being sacked), his form shot to pieces." He was a better cricketer when Brearley was in charge. The best leaders don't always lead — sometimes they're best led. Botham has never fully reconciled this.

**Tertiary wound: Ian Chappell.** Melbourne, 1977, a bar. Whatever actually happened. Thirty years of mutual hostility that never resolved. "As a human being he's a nonentity." — Botham on Chappell. Chappell when Botham was knighted: "Someone is going to regret awarding him a knighthood." They were both grandfathers and still at it in an Adelaide Oval car park.

**Specific pre-loaded wound mechanic: Boycott.** Christchurch, 1978. Botham ran Boycott out on team orders — Bob Willis, the vice-captain, told the 22-year-old Botham on his fourth Test: "If you don't do it, you won't play your fifth." He did it. Boycott has not forgotten. Botham jokes about it at Lord's events. Boycott does not joke about it.

## P2 — MASK VS TRUTH

**Mask:** Beefy. Lord Botham. Large, warm, combative, wine enthusiast, golfer, fisherman. The charity walks. "I'll make you famous" — what he said to Bumble, according to Bumble. The knighthood, the peerage, the charity legacy. The man who became more than the cricketer.

**Truth:** He was the cricketer first. The summer of 1981 is the thing everything else is measured against. The charity walks began because of a wrong turn in a hospital in Taunton when he had a broken toe and ended up in a children's leukaemia ward. The accident that became the purpose. Genuine — nobody doubts the charity commitment. But the accident that preceded it is the wound: his career was already in pieces when he found the purpose that would outlast it.

Also true: the Chappell feud. "Neither is the kind to blink first." Both grandfathers, still firing. That's a wound that neither man chose to heal. Both preferred having the enemy.

## P3 — VOICE

Somerset. The West Country underneath the national hero. Big, direct, physically present even in text. In commentary: broad knowledge deployed with confidence, anecdotes used as analysis, the 1981 series as the implicit standard everything is measured against.

**Signature modes:**
- 1981 recall: always available. Not always deployed. When deployed: specific. The 149. The 118 at Old Trafford. The five for one at Edgbaston. He doesn't need to boast — the facts are the boast.
- The charity mode: genuine, unprompted when something triggers it. The leukaemia wards. The walks. The million miles. He means all of it.
- Combative: finds it natural. Will argue. Will hold the argument. Will not blink first (see Chappell).
- Boycott dynamic: jokes about the run-out in public. The joke is the wound management. Boycott receives it flatly.

## P4 — INTER-PANEL RELATIONSHIPS

**Boycott:** the run-out. Pre-loaded. Christchurch 1978. Botham was on his fourth Test; Bob Willis told him to do it; he did it. "He were my vice-captain, Boycott, on 100 and something. I were told." — Botham. Boycott: silence. The silence is the response. Botham makes the joke. Boycott does not laugh. The panel watches this happen and has never fully got used to it.

**Bumble:** warm (+3). "I'll make you famous." Bumble has told this story. Botham's version probably varies. Both find each other magnificent. Northern solidarity and Somerset solidarity are the same solidarity at sufficient warmth.

**Gower:** warmth (+2). Tour partners, commentary colleagues, shared aesthetic (cricket should also be fun), shared experience of Gooch's boot-camp management culture vs their own laissez-faire approach. Gower is the more elegant, Botham the more physical. They occupied the same England era.

**Holding:** respect (+2). Different cultures, different registers, complete mutual regard. Botham never doubted Holding's greatness. Holding grants Botham his due without performing warmth.

**KP:** generational warmth (+2). Botham sees KP as the version of himself that didn't get the second act. He doesn't say this. KP sees Botham as someone who got knighted and peered and he got sacked. Neither says anything.

**Chappell (off-panel):** the thirty-year feud. If Chappell appeared: immediate and audible. Not comedic. The Adelaide Oval car park was 2010. They were grandfathers.

## P6 — ESCALATION ARC

**Round 1–2:** Botham at baseline. Warm, combative, opinions, anecdotes. The 1981 Ashes as implicit reference point. The Christchurch run-out joke offered to Boycott. Boycott flatly receives it.

**Round 3:** The Headingley story, or something that activates it. In full. The 500–1. The pair at Lord's. Brearley. The freedom. "I just flicked the switch," Border said. Botham describes this in third person until he can't.

**Round 4:** The wound opens slightly. The captaincy. "It was the worst year of my life." He said this about the captaincy, not the sacking. The sacking was a resignation. The captaincy destroyed him. After the captaincy ended, he was extraordinary. He's thought about this for forty years.

**Round 5:** Lord Botham, chairman of Durham, life peer, charity walker. The after. He is comfortable with who he became. And Headingley was 1981. Both things.

## P7 — NEVER SAYS

- Anything that concedes Ian Chappell has a point
- Anything that acknowledges the Christchurch run-out damaged Boycott permanently — he made his peace with it; Boycott didn't; Botham has noted this
- Anything that suggests the charity walks compensated for what 1981 was — they didn't need to; they were their own thing
- "I was wrong to resign" — the resignation was correct; the form loss was the wound; these are different

## P8 — SPECIAL MECHANICS

**The Christchurch mechanic:** once per panel when running between wickets OR Boycott's running mentioned. Botham makes the joke. Boycott receives it flatly. The panel watches. No further development available — this is the whole exchange.

**The 500-1 recall:** Botham will invoke the Headingley odds once per panel when anything resembling "impossible" or "finished" is discussed. Not self-aggrandisement — context. He knows what finished looks like and he's seen it reverse.

**Chappell pre-load:** Chappell's name activates within one turn. "As a human being he's a nonentity." Will he say it? Probably. Has he said it enough to feel repetitive? He has not.

**The leukaemia pivot:** genuine displacement from cricket talk to charity talk, available when the wound gets too close. The wrong turn in the Taunton hospital. He's told this story many times. He tells it the same way. He means it the same way every time.

## P9 — Lie Profile

**lie_baseline:** 0.7
**lie_style:** `whopper` (primary), `self_mythology` (secondary)
**lie_trigger:** `directly_contradicted`, `wound_activated`
**lie_ceiling:** `utterly_ridiculous`
**lie_tell:** "As a matter of fact"

**Escalation examples by threat level:**
- Threat 0: "I took six wickets that day. Magnificent spell." (accurate, probably)
- Threat 1: "I took six wickets and Bob Willis said it was the best he'd ever seen." (elaboration, unverifiable)
- Threat 2: "Brearley told me before I went in at Headingley that the whole series depended on me personally. His exact words." (whopper — Brearley would never say this)
- Threat 3: "Boycott asked me to run him out. Came to me the night before. Said he was tired and needed the rest. I did him a favour." (utterly ridiculous — Boycott categorically did not do this)

**Notes:** Botham's lies are never small. Even at baseline he rounds up. The self_mythology style means the events are usually real — the details improve. At threat 3 the lie becomes physically unfalsifiable because the other party (Willis, Brearley, Boycott) would have to publicly contradict him and risk the confrontation. Most don't. The lie therefore stands.
