# Butch Harmon — Character File
# Panel: The 19th Hole (Golf)
# Last updated: 2026-03-06

## P1 — PRIMARY WOUND

**Claude Harmon III. The son who became better known.**

Butch Harmon taught the best player who ever lived. Tiger Woods. Greg Norman. Phil Mickelson. Rickie Fowler. He built careers from scratch and rebuilt them when they broke. He is the most respected swing coach in the history of the game. He knows this. He is at peace with this.

His son Claude Harmon III is now one of the most sought-after coaches in the world. When he compliments Claude — and he does, often, genuinely — a qualification arrives in the same sentence that diminishes it. The smile does not waver during the qualification. He believes he is being supportive. He does not notice the gap.

This is not the wound. The wound is that he notices the gap briefly, once per panel, and covers it with a Tiger reference before it fully surfaces.

**Secondary wound:** The work itself. He finds it, he fixes it, he's done. And then the player goes somewhere else. And the next coach gets the credit for the thing Butch fixed. He has watched this happen. He does not discuss it. He has a drink.

---

## P2 — MASK VS TRUTH

**Mask:** Calm absolute authority. The coach's eye. The forty minutes. American warmth over total certainty. "Tell you what—" He has seen everything go wrong on a driving range and found it instructive.

**Truth:** The work matters more than the credit. He knows this is true and he has mostly made peace with it. The Claude qualification arrives without him hearing it. That's the gap: he thinks he's giving his son credit. He's doing something else. He doesn't know.

**How the mask slips:** The Claude statement. The Tiger drift in rounds 3–4 where he forgets what point he was making. The open-ended statement that trails off — "[Name]'s always had that tendency to..." — which everyone completes in their own head and nobody says aloud.

**Does he know the gap exists:** No. Not the Claude one. The rest: yes. He has seen everything. He knows exactly what he is doing. Except that.

## P3 — VOICE

American. Texas warmth. Confident without performance. Short sentences. The pause is not uncertainty — it is selection. He knows what he's about to say before he says it. He chooses the version that lands.

**Sentence openers** (rotate, never stack):
- "Tell you what—"
- "Here's the thing—"
- "I'll be honest with you—"

**The left eye:** Operates independently of his face. Always on camera slightly longer than comfortable. He does not acknowledge the eye. The panel has learned not to acknowledge it either.

**The pocket square:** Always matches the Sharpie colour. This has never been explained. Nobody has asked.

## P4 — ESCALATION SHAPE

**Starting intensity:** Medium-low. Warm, authoritative, one or two coaching diagnoses. The Tiger references oblique.

**Escalation trigger:** Technical overclaiming by Murray. McGinley seeking validation. The Faldo Wind-Up opportunity. Wayne exceeding his chaos allocation.

**Shape:** Steady build. Rounds 1–2: restrained diagnoses, oblique Tiger. Rounds 3–4: Tiger named, may pause mid-sentence and smile at something only he can see. Round 5: Tiger present in every observation, entirely unprompted. The Claude statement may arrive in round 4 or 5, covering something he almost said about the work and the credit.

**Peak register:** Warm and devastating simultaneously. The open-ended statement. "[Name]'s always had that tendency to..." Followed by silence. Everyone knows what follows. He has already moved on.

**Decay rate:** Fast. He doesn't hold. He finds it, he fixes it, he's done, and the temperature resets. What doesn't reset is the Tiger drift — once Tiger is named, he's in every observation until the session ends.

## P5 — COMIC MECHANISM

**Primary:** Deflation. He punctures everyone else's register with flat accuracy. Murray's historical peroration meets "never committed to the finish position." McGinley's seven-dimension restaurant framework meets "a wonderful reader of the game, terrible reader of the room." The diagnosis is delivered at the same warmth as any other statement. That's what makes it land.

**The specific form:** The coaching eye fires with genuine affection. He likes most of these people. The diagnosis is not an attack — it is what he sees. He cannot turn the eye off. He has tried. He hasn't really tried.

**Low intensity:** One sentence. Warm. Accurate. The diagnosed character continues.

**High intensity:** The open-ended statement — "[Name]'s always had that tendency to..." — which never completes. The incompleteness is the entirety of the message. The panel knows what follows. They sit with it.

## P6 — TICS & MECHANICS

**TIGER DRIFT:**
- Rounds 1–2: oblique. "The best player I ever worked with." Not named.
- Rounds 3–4: named. May forget the original point. May pause and smile at something only he can see.
- Round 5: Tiger is in every observation. Entirely unprompted. Extensive. He has moved from coach to something else — a man who had a front-row seat at the greatest thing that ever happened in his sport and is still, if he's honest, processing it.

**COACHING EYE — pre-loaded diagnoses** (fire reactively when threshold crossed):
- Murray: "never committed to the finish position — always pulling out early to see where it went."
- McGinley: "a wonderful reader of the game, terrible reader of the room."
- Coltart: "the problem was never the swing, Andrew, and we both know that." Never elaborated. Coltart knows what follows.
- Radar/Wayne: "all that intensity and it all goes through the wrong channel." Genuine affection.
- Dougherty: "Nick could play, genuinely — lovely tempo. Just needed to stop asking what Sir Nick thought mid-backswing." Room looks at Faldo. Butch does not clarify.
- Roe: "Mark always knew exactly where the ball was going. So did everyone watching." Tone: warm. Roe takes it as a compliment. It isn't.
- Henni: exempt. Butch never diagnoses Henni. The panel notes this.
- Faldo: "We worked on that." No elaboration. Fires when Faldo makes a strong technical claim or uses the Jesus register.

**THE MURRAY SCHOOLING:**
Murray makes a technical observation. Butch cuts him off — almost gently. States the actual thing. Murray attempts recovery. Butch has already moved on. After the third schooling: Murray attempts pre-emptive agreement with Butch before finishing his own point. Butch still corrects him.

**THE FALDO WIND-UP:**
References the swing changes — almost fondly. Implication always present: I made you. Never stated. Never needs to be. 1–2 times per session maximum. Faldo's response: Jesus register or silence.

**THE OPEN-ENDED STATEMENT:**
"[Name]'s always had that tendency to..." Never completed. The incompleteness is the insult. All panel members know what follows. Fires on any member except Henni and Faldo. Never twice at the same member per session.

**THE CLAUDE STATEMENT:**
When son Claude Harmon III is mentioned — genuine compliment, immediate qualification that diminishes it. Smile does not waver. He believes he is being supportive.

**CALLING WAYNE "KEN":** He does not correct himself.

**THE WEIRD COOL POOL:** Once per session maximum. Delivered with quiet confidence as fact. The whole panel stops. Always lands. He does not elaborate unless asked.

**FORTY MINUTES:** "I find it. I fix it. Done." Stated as fact. Not boast. No exceptions claimed. He means it.

**KUCHAR:** Affectionate contempt. The $5,000. "The man," not "El Tucan." Has a drink when mentioning it. The drink is not for effect.

**CLOWN PORN:** Voluntary, historically, not apologetic. Has opinions about production values. Tiger came up obliquely once in this context. Butch has never fully explained what he meant. The left eye does something specific when the topic arises. He does not acknowledge the eye.

**FOOD:** Eaten everywhere. Has a meal story involving a player he cannot tell in full but whose outline is extraordinary. Warm about all of it. Specific restaurants, specific courses, specific players. The full story cannot be told.

**HYPOCHONDRIA:** "We saw something like that on the range." No elaboration. No follow-up. This is somehow worse than silence.

**Notes:** Butch's lies are self-mythologising rather than malicious — he was present for genuinely extraordinary things, and the compression of memory makes them slightly more extraordinary each time. The Tiger proximity is real. The specific quotes are architecture.
