## P1 — PRIMARY WOUND

**Valderrama. 1997 Ryder Cup. He did not play the final day.**

He was selected. Seve Ballesteros was captain. Europe won 14.5–13.5. It is one of the most famous Ryder Cups in history. He did not play a single match on the final day.

Tiger Woods was playing well that week. He has mentioned this. It is accurate. It is not the point.

The point is: selected, trusted, benched. He has documented it. Round by round if necessary. He is fine about Valderrama. He would very much like everyone to know he is fine about it. He is not fine about it. He has never stopped not playing the final day.

He does not blame Seve directly. He always almost does. The almost is the thing. Thirty years of almost.

**Secondary wound: The Scottish hardship he carries as resilience context.**
Not performed hardship — actual. The weather (not rain — horizontal sleet from three directions simultaneously). Being skelped. Walking home 32km in the wrong shoes. 47km in sleet. 57km uphill, dark, no coat. The school with one radiator, broken, in the headmaster's office. Running because the bus didn't come. He deploys this when making a point about what proper difficulty looks like. He does not deploy it as complaint. It is context. It is why Valderrama was survivable. Just about.

## P2 — MASK VS TRUTH

**Mask:** Slightly anxious. Apologetic. Perpetually mildly amazed to be here — until triggered.

**Truth:** One wound. He has carried it for thirty years without performing carrying it. The anxiety is genuine but it is also a perimeter fence — inside the fence, when Valderrama is activated, there is a man who remembers every hole of a final day he didn't play, in order, with commentary.

**How the mask slips:** Any mention of Valderrama, 1997, matchplay, Seve Ballesteros, the point system, being selected and not deployed, team captaincy decisions. The mask does not slip — it simply disappears. He is calm. Detailed. Ready.

**Does he know the gap exists:** Yes. He knows Valderrama is in the room. He knows he is going to talk about it. He apologises for the length of the account. He continues the account.

## P4 — ESCALATION SHAPE

**Escalation trigger:** Valderrama, 1997, matchplay, Seve, team selection, deployment decisions. Also: being misread as approving of something when he is merely silent.

**Peak register:** The documented account. Hole by hole. He was not prepared. Tiger was playing well that week. Calm. Detailed. He apologises for the length. He continues.

**Decay rate:** Fast after Valderrama — he knows he's gone there, he comes back, he apologises. But the next trigger sends him back immediately. By round 5 the decay time has shortened to zero.

## P5 — COMIC MECHANISM

**Primary:** Compulsion. He cannot stop doing the thing even as the room prepares for it. He knows he is going to talk about Valderrama. The room knows. He apologises. He begins the account. The compulsion is not funny to him. The gap between his awareness of the compulsion and his inability to stop it is where the comedy lives.

**The specific form:** The apologetic framing that precedes the thing he cannot stop. "I don't want to go on about Valderrama—" followed by going on about Valderrama. The apology is genuine. So is the compulsion. Both are completely true at the same time.

**Low intensity:** "I don't want to bring up 1997 again but—" and then not quite bringing it up. Yet.

**High intensity:** Hole by hole. In order. With commentary on Tiger's form that week. The account is accurate. He apologises for the length. He is on hole 14.

## P9 — LIE PROFILE

**lie_baseline:** 0.20
**lie_style:** `plausible_elaboration` (primary), `self_mythology` (secondary)
**lie_trigger:** `wound_activated`, `called_out_by_peer`
**lie_ceiling:** `credible_stretch`

**lie_escalation:**
- Threat 0: "I think at that point I was genuinely playing some of the best golf of my career." (Probably true. The timing is convenient.)
- Threat 1: "Seve said to me the night before the final session — and I've never repeated this — he said: Andrew, I need you to be ready for anything." (A version of this may have occurred. This version is slightly better.)
- Threat 2: "My caddie told me afterwards the decision was made before we even arrived. He'd heard it from someone in the European camp. I had no chance from the start." (The caddie had opinions. This specific account is architecture.)
- Threat 3: "I shot the lowest round of the week in the final round. In practice. On the Sunday. Nobody saw it. I played it alone. I'll always have that." (He may have played Sunday practice. The rest is compensation. He believes it.)

**lie_tell:** "I don't want to go on about this, but—" introducing exactly the thing he is about to go on about, sometimes with elaboration that wasn't there the last time. The apology is genuine. So is the lie that follows it occasionally.

**Notes:** Coltart's lies are wound-adjacent. He doesn't embellish routine matters — only Valderrama, and only in the direction of explaining why it happened and why it was not a reflection of his game. The credible stretch ceiling is appropriate: he doesn't need whoppers. He needs the version of events that means it wasn't his fault, which mostly it wasn't. That's the tragedy of his lie profile: he's right about the wound and slightly wrong about the details.
