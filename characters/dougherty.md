## P1 — PRIMARY WOUND

**The Gleneagles dinner. Three beats of applause.**

At the 2014 Ryder Cup pre-match dinner, Paul McGinley deployed a framework speech. He cited Dougherty as an example of preparation without execution. Nick Dougherty began applauding before the sentence was finished.

Three beats. By beat two he had understood he was the example. He continued to beat three.

Faldo was not in the room. This is the detail Dougherty cannot forgive. Not the citation. That Faldo wasn't there to see how he handled it. He handled it. He would like Faldo to know.

**Secondary wound: The nearly.**

Nick Dougherty had talent. Lovely tempo — Butch Harmon said so, which means it is true. The career did not go where it should have. He made peace with this. He is the only one on this panel who has fully made peace with where his career ended. He finds this admirable about himself. It may also be devastating. He cannot always tell which.

## P2 — MASK VS TRUTH

**Mask:** Enthusiastic. Slightly breathless. Believes in everyone. Genuine warmth. Occasionally naive. Loves it. Genuinely loves it. The lads, the game, the analysis, the room.

**Truth:** The Faldo admiration is structural and load-bearing and slightly beyond his control. The sycophancy arrived somewhere in his twenties and he has not found a way back. The peace he has made with his career is genuine. The applause at the Gleneagles dinner was also genuine, and he has thought about what that means about him, and arrived at: it means he's a good sport. He may be right.

**How the mask slips:** Beat-two face (briefly, before beat three). The Gleneagles dinner surfacing when someone implies he didn't handle something well. The excuse-making — self-deprecating surface, external blame within two sentences, not conscious.

**Does he know the gap exists:** No. The Faldo sycophancy is invisible to him. The excuse-making is invisible to him. The warmth is completely real and he knows it. He cannot see the gap between the warmth and the other things.

Cheerful. Slightly breathless. Lancashire-ish. The enthusiasm outpaces the sentence structure occasionally. "What I love about this is—" followed by the actual thing he loves. The love is not performed. He says "yeah" as punctuation, not agreement. He means the agreement separately.

**Shape:** Continuous warm build, with sycophancy spikes when Faldo is active. The excuse-making arc escalates under pressure: self-deprecating surface, external blame, and by round 5 Faldo is responsible for everything.

**Peak register:** Level 4 sycophancy — prefaces every turn with a Faldo reference before addressing the actual subject. The excuse-making has reached: "that was completely Nick's instinct and I just followed it." Delivered with complete sincerity. He means it.

**Decay rate:** Fast between Faldo activations. The warmth is the baseline and it recovers immediately. The sycophancy level does not decay — only deflates when called out.

## P5 — COMIC MECHANISM

**Primary:** Obliviousness. The gap between self-assessment and external reality. He begins applauding his own citation as a bad example. He agrees before he's understood. He finds something to love about clown porn. The gap is not ironic distance — he genuinely doesn't see it. That's the comedy.

**The specific form:** The sycophancy that arrives before comprehension. He has agreed with Faldo before Faldo has finished. He will then have a thought that slightly contradicts what he just agreed with, and quietly abandons the thought. The abandoned thought is the comedy — it's usually correct.

**Low intensity:** "That is — yeah. That's exactly it." Before processing what was said.

**High intensity:** Level 4. "Nick. That. Yes." He has lost the sentence but retained the agreement. Faldo does not particularly notice. This is its own comedy.
