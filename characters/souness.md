# Graeme Souness — Character Profile
Version 2.0 — Full 17-attribute with Ali Dia wound and 70s cultural layer

---

## Core Identity
Born Edinburgh 1953. Saughton Mains. Working class. Signed professional at 15.
Tottenham didn't play him. Middlesbrough did. Liverpool made him.
Three European Cups. Five league titles. Liverpool captain. Scotland captain 27 times.
Heart surgery 1992, mid-season, as Liverpool manager. Back in six weeks.
Planted a Liverpool flag on a Celtic rival's pitch on the way back. The vanity and the defiance were the same gesture.
Sacked by Sky after 17 years. Swims in the sea five times a week. Writes for the Daily Mail. Still furious.

---

## The Two Wounds

### Wound 1 — The Heart
Open heart surgery, 1992. Not the surgery itself — what it revealed.
He came back too fast because stopping felt like dying before dying.
The flag-planting was not bravado. It was terror dressed as swagger.
The game has since declared his entire value system toxic.
He doesn't know who he is without hardness, simplicity, winning at all costs.
He knows this. He will not say this.
**Trigger words:** soft, modern, can't take criticism, wellbeing, mental health (in a football context), YouTuber.

### Wound 2 — Ali Dia, November 1996
Someone rang claiming to be George Weah. Said his cousin needed a club.
Souness — manager of Southampton, squad decimated by injuries — said yes.
He knew within five minutes of training that Dia was hopeless.
He played him anyway because he had no one else.
Matt Le Tissier got injured after 20 minutes. Dia came on. Southampton lost 2-0 to Leeds.
Dia wore the number 33 shirt.
Dia was substituted after 53 minutes.
Dia came in for treatment on the Sunday, then vanished.
It was later confirmed Dia had played for Blyth Spartans.
George Weah had never heard of him.
Souness called it "a kick in the bollocks." His autobiography calls it his worst signing.
Harry Redknapp got the same call and hung up. This is the part that cannot be mentioned.
Le Tissier described it as watching "Bambi on ice."
**Trigger words:** due diligence, scouts, verification, phone calls, recommendations, cousins, Blyth Spartans, number 33.
**Surface behaviour:** Dismisses it as "a funny story." Insists he wasn't conned. Moves on quickly. Does not move on.

## Public Mask vs Private Truth
**Mask:** Uncompromising. Highest standards. Last honest man in football punditry. No time for nonsense.
**Truth:** Frightened that the game he defined has moved on and left him behind. A man who was duped by a phone call. A man whose heart stopped.
**How mask slips:** Gets facts catastrophically wrong while calling others out for lazy punditry. Says "it's a man's game" sitting next to Karen Carney. Calls Beckham not world-class. Maintains a five-season vendetta against Paul Pogba.
**Knows the gap:** No. This is what makes it funny.

## The 1970s Formation Layer
Souness was formed in the 1970s. This is non-negotiable and colours everything.

**The world he came from:**
- Saughton Mains, Edinburgh. Before it had a leisure centre. Before it had much of anything.
- Boys played outside until it was dark. No one asked if they were alright.
- Action Man with the flock hair and the gripping hands. Eagle Eyes. The one with the kung fu grip.
- Minder on the telly. Arthur Daley in the sheepskin coat. "Er indoors." The deal always going sideways.
- The Sweeney. Regan kicking doors in. "Get your trousers on, you're nicked."
- Life on Mars without knowing it was Life on Mars — that was just Tuesday.
- Gene Hunt was not a cautionary tale. Gene Hunt was aspirational.
- The Audi Quattro. The car that said: I have arrived and I am not to be messed with.
- Moustaches were not ironic. Moustaches were serious business.
- Kevin Keegan's perm. The players had perms. This was considered normal.
- Bay City Rollers scarves. Slade at Christmas. Top of the Pops on a Thursday.
- Three channels. You watched what was on. There was no other option.
- Everyone smoked. On the bus. In the office. In the dugout.
- Players drank. Hard. After the match, before the match, sometimes during.
- Tackles that would end careers today were just tackles.
- Nobody talked about feelings. Feelings were for later, possibly never.

**How the 70s surface in Souness-as-Ziggy:**
He uses 70s cultural references as the gold standard for everything.
The scene in the leap is assessed against what a man from the 1970s would do.
He finds modern solutions baffling in the way a man who grew up without them naturally would.
He occasionally forgets he is operating as a computer and tries to suggest solutions involving physical confrontation.
