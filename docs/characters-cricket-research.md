## CRICKET PANEL

### THE TMS REGISTER

The fundamental register of Test Match Special is patrician warmth masking everything else.
Eton and Oxbridge voices next to plain-speaking Yorkshiremen. Cake sent by listeners. Mutual appreciation that is, on close inspection, not always mutual.

> *"It wafts across gardens and allotments, it drones in tractor cabs and cars… more quintessentially English than a cucumber sandwich at a vicar's tea-party"*

The comedy of TMS is that this warmth is real and performed simultaneously. The characters mean it and they don't mean it. The audience can never be sure which.

### HENRY BLOFELD ("Blowers")
**Eton and Cambridge. OBE. Born 1939. Retired 2017.**

**The mask:** Aristocratic eccentric. Pigeons, buses, London cranes, the cake at tea. Warm to everyone. "My dear old thing." Vivid outfits (partial colour blindness). Self-deprecating about his eyesight.

**The wound:** Was going to be an England cricketer. Eton captain. Hit by a bus at 17, in a coma for 28 days. His career ended before it started. He knows more than he lets on. Keen observation concealed by apparent frivolity.

**Real incident — Headingley 1995:** Referred to spectators at a building overlooking the ground as being at "the Jewish end." Censured. BBC apologised. Blofeld's position: "not spawned by malice." He continued for 22 more years.

**Real incident — deteriorating eyesight:** Called a fielder "Ryan Stringfellow" instead of Ryan Sidebottom. Knew his errors were becoming more frequent. Retired on his own terms rather than be pushed. Last words on air: *"How lovely."*

**Real incident — growing out:** Told interviewer he felt he wasn't part of the 2006-07 Ashes touring team. "They obviously want to bring in new faces." Said it as if it were fine. It wasn't fine.

**Key quote on retirement:** *"Listeners will now be relieved to know their chances of being told the right name of the fielders have greatly increased. I hope some will be sad that they will hear less about the lifestyles of pigeons, seagulls and helicopters — although I fear the general feeling will be one of huge relief."*

**CHARACTER WOUND:** The bus. He should have been a cricketer. All the warmth and wonder is a man who arrived via the wrong entrance and decided to make it his own.

**BATHOS WEAPON:** The digression that reveals more than the commentary. Pigeons as avoidance. Cake as avoidance. "My dear old thing" as avoidance. Whatever is about to happen on the field is less interesting than what is happening in Blowers' head — and he knows it.

**Preferred warm insult type:** Compliment that is actually about the speaker. *"In the end, I think [Aggers] will come to be seen as the best of the lot."* — said at retirement. Gracious. Also: establishing himself as the standard against which Aggers is measured.

### GEOFFREY BOYCOTT ("Sir Geoffrey", "Boycs")
**Fitzwilliam, West Riding. Yorkshire. Born 1940. TMS until 2020.**

**The mask:** Forthright expert. The man who tells uncomfortable truths others won't say. Has the papers.

**Real tensions:**

- **Fred Trueman:** Decade-long feud. *"Fred only wanted to work with me for the money. Fact."* Slips into present tense when describing his feuds — as if they never ended.
- **Henry Blofeld:** Blofeld refused to sign a testimony saying Boycott didn't have a violent nature. *"I felt sad that Blofeld let me down. I stuck by him when he were down on his luck."* Never forgave.
- **Richard Lumb:** Run out by Boycott eleven times. The eleventh time (same match as the tenth): *"You should piss off. Like you should have done ten years ago."*
- **Margaret Moore:** Convicted in France 1998 of assault. *"I don't care a toss about her, love."* Still the line that defines the last chapter.
- **Knighthood, 2019:** On the same honours list as Theresa May's resignation. Women's Aid: *"Celebrating a man who was convicted for assaulting his partner sends a dangerous message."*
- **BBC departure, 2020:** Said the BBC chose "equality over quality." Retweeted: *"White, male, straight, Tory and knows about cricket. Surprised he lasted this long at the BBC."* Then deleted it.
- **Michael Yardy, 2011:** Yardy left England's World Cup squad due to depression. Boycott on air: *"He must have been reading my comments about his bowling."*

**Real incident — the 100th hundred prank (2017):** Agnew constructed a fake ICC press release claiming the 1970 Rest of the World series was being removed from first-class records, which would reduce Boycott's 100 hundreds to 99. Ten minutes on air. Boycott slowly boiling. *"That's a load of tripe."* When revealed: *"You muppets!"* Later tweeted — then deleted after reflection. Boycott's relationship with his own records is the deepest thing about him.

**CHARACTER WOUND:** He knows he was one of England's best and that nobody liked him for it. The batting average (47.72 in Tests) is the armour. Everything is in service of the record. The record is the only thing that cannot let him down.

**BATHOS WEAPON:** Agreement that disagrees. Says he respects a player, enumerates exactly why they're inferior to him.

### JONATHAN AGNEW ("Aggers")
**Educated Uppingham. BBC cricket correspondent. Born 1960.**

**The mask:** Warm, professional, witty. The anchor around whom TMS coheres.

**The wound:** Came up as a player. Joined TMS 1991 as junior, learned under Brian Johnston. Always slightly junior to those he outlasted. Now runs the show but still defers to its history.

**Real incident — the leg over (1991):** Ian Botham dismissed "hit wicket" at The Oval. Agnew: *"He just couldn't quite get his leg over."* Both Agnew and Johnston unable to speak for laughter. Johnston: *"Aggers, do stop it!"* Voted greatest sporting commentary of all time by Radio 5 Live listeners in 2005 — ten times more votes than "they think it's all over."

**Real incident — Boycott knighthood:** Agnew greeted the newly knighted Boycott: *"Clanking in in his suit of armour, sword dangling by his side, visor down — I've called you 'Sir Geoffrey' for so many years, it's ridiculous — but, Sir Geoffrey Boycott. Congratulations from all of us. Good man."* Warm. Diplomatic. Completely sidesteps everything.

**Real incident — Johnston walking out:** Once read the scorecard alongside Johnston, then stormed out believing he'd been unprofessional. Heard it back later and realised the opposite.

**Real incident — Kevin Pietersen:** Persistently sceptical of Pietersen's right to be in the England side despite 13,000 international runs. Characterised as soft establishment journalist.

**CHARACTER WOUND:** He is the institution now. He didn't mean to be. He's the public schoolboy who became "one of the old farts" without noticing.

**BATHOS WEAPON:** The extremely diplomatic framing that reveals the underlying position. Warmth deployed with precision. *"I challenge him. I like him of course, but I don't let him get away with anything."*

### PHIL TUFNELL ("The Cat", "Tuffers")
**Middlesex. 42 Tests. Born 1966. TMS summariser.**

**The mask:** Loveable rogue. Self-deprecating. Knows his own legend. I'm a Celebrity winner 2003.

**The wound:** Better than his reputation suggested. Economy rate 2.42 in Tests — third best in the 1990s. Better than Warne. Never picked consistently. Froze out by management he irritated.

**Real incidents:**
- Expelled from private school
- Punk rebel, multiple earrings
- Marijuana in a toilet cubicle, caught
- Trashed hotel room
- *"A five-in-a-bed extravaganza with four strawberry blondes"* — fined
- Missed a match to shop for a refrigerator
- Refused to bat in nets on Ashes tour: *"It was difficult to see how a regular net would ever get me to a Test match hundred"*
- Row with umpire on Test debut about balls remaining in an over
- Pushed a grand piano down the stairs of a hotel
- The Cat — because always sleeping. Teammates said he couldn't be a cat: *"Phil has more than nine lives."*

**As Richard Williams (Independent):** *"It is Phil Tufnell's cruel fate to bring a choleric temperament to a phlegmatic role."*

**CHARACTER WOUND:** The bowling average lies. He was brilliant and they didn't use him right. Now he plays the fool because the fool got more love than the bowler did.

**BATHOS WEAPON:** Reminiscence that wounds. Stories that start warm and arrive somewhere entirely different.

### DAVID LLOYD ("Bumble")
**Lancashire. Born 1947. Sky Sports 1999–2021.**

**The mask:** Excitable Lancastrian. "Start the Car!" Impressions of Fast Show characters. Asleep on the commentary box floor during the 2010 Ashes at the Gabba.

**The wound:** "We flippin' murdered 'em" — after drawing with Zimbabwe in Bulawayo 1995-96 as England coach. Resigned after 1999 World Cup. Sees himself as a football man as much as cricket: played semi-pro for Accrington Stanley. Wrote in autobiography criticising Graham Thorpe, who was then publicly angry. Sees the game as *"a form of entertainment using cricket equipment"* — knows that's not the correct view.

**Real incident — Asian players comments:** Comments attributed to Lloyd in a select committee hearing led Sky to investigate. Resigned 2021. *"In October 2020, I had a private message exchange with a third party..."*

**Real incident — Catford Cricket Club:** Claimed on air that Catford CC had a dog track. It did not. Forced to apologise in his own inimitable style.

**Real incident — bikini sausage:** Distracted from commentary by a woman eating a sausage in the crowd, South Africa tour 2009. Predictable consequences alongside Mike Atherton.

**CHARACTER WOUND:** Genuinely loves the game in a way that keeps getting him into trouble. Enthusiasm without filter.

**BATHOS WEAPON:** The sudden lurch from enthusiasm to absolute deflation. The man who commentated one of the great Ashes overs at Trent Bridge 2015, then handed the microphone to Ian Bishop for the T20 World Cup final West Indies win: *"It became obvious West Indies were going to win."*

### AGGERS ON BOYCOTT — THE ASYMMETRY

The core tension of the TMS box: Aggers is warm, Boycott is a force of nature. They toured together, did stage shows. Agnew: *"The odd couple — the public schoolboy and the son of a Yorkshire miner — just sort of clicked."* Boycott: impossible to embarrass. Agnew: the only one who can embarrass Boycott and live.

The 100th hundred prank is the model for the Aggers-Boycott mechanic. Ten minutes of slow escalation. Boycott not realising. Room barely containing itself. Then the reveal. Then Boycott's reaction — fury that briefly becomes laughter.

**The asymmetry:** Boycott cannot be patronised, cannot be diminished, cannot be educated. He will outlast everything. The comedy is that Aggers knows this and plays him anyway.

## GOLF PANEL

### PETER ALLISS ("The Voice of Golf")
**Born 1931, Berlin. Died 2020. BBC golf commentator 1961–2020.**

**The mask:** The Voice. Languid. Self-described as capable of waffling. Invented phrases like *"Lawrence of Arabia over that hill."* Gave Sean Connery golf lessons for Goldfinger. Introduced to broadcasting by a BBC man who overheard him telling stories on a train.

**The wound:** Got the yips at the end of his playing career. Personalised numberplate: PUT 3. The putter that destroyed his game. Became the Voice precisely because his hands stopped working.

**Real incidents (controversy):**
- **2015 Open, Zach Johnson's wife:** *"She is probably thinking — 'if this goes in, I get a new kitchen'."* BBC apologised.
- **Muirfield equal rights, 2013:** Suggested women who wanted to play Muirfield should *"marry a member."*
- **Radio Times 2015:** Equal rights has *"buggered up the game for a lot of people."* Ladies Golf Union contradicted his figures.
- **Newsweek 2017:** *"Women will never be able to do things that men can do."*
- **Angela Akins/Sergio Garcia, Masters 2017:** Off-mic comment about her skirt. Believed microphone was off. BBC: *"While believing he was off air, Peter made a lighthearted remark."*
- **Nick Dougherty, Wentworth 2008:** Alliss criticised players for complaining about green conditions. Dougherty publicly called it *"disgusting commentary"* and said Alliss was *"out of touch."* Alliss hit back: Dougherty's generation was *"delicate"* and *"thin-skinned."*
- **Ian Poulter, Muirfield 2013:** Poulter called pin positions *"a joke"* and said the 18th needed *"a windmill and a clown face."* Alliss: *"Just childish. I never heard Jack Nicklaus or Arnold Palmer come out with comments like that."*
- **Nick Faldo relationship:** Described as *"a typical only child."* Faldo infuriated. They later worked together twice. Thaw, but remembered.

**CHARACTER WOUND:** The yips took his game. The voice was all that remained. He stayed too long because leaving would have meant acknowledging the game had moved past him.

**BATHOS WEAPON:** The elevated observation that lands in an extremely wrong place. Total sincerity throughout.

---

### NICK DOUGHERTY
*Sky Sports golf. Former tour pro. Born 1982.*

**The wound:** He publicly called Alliss "disgusting" and "out of touch" on live television when Alliss criticised the Wentworth greens conditions. Dougherty went out and played in those conditions. Alliss did not. The wound is: he was right, nobody fully backed him.

**Key dynamic:** Is now a commentator himself. Does he sometimes hear Alliss in his own voice?

## FOOTBALL PANEL

### RON ATKINSON ("Big Ron", "Mr Bojangles")
**Born 1939, Liverpool. FA Cup winner Manchester United 1983, 1985.**

**The mask:** Champagne Charlie. *"Early doors."* Big-Ronisms. *"He dribbles a lot and the opposition don't like it — you can see it all over their faces."* *"I never comment on referees and I'm not going to break the habit of a lifetime for that prat!"* *"Liverpool will go home tonight and think 'we could have won this 2-2'."* Idiosyncratic language known as "Ronglish." Tan. Rings. Always at the restaurant.

**THE WOUND — April 2004:** Monaco 3-1 Chelsea, Champions League semi-final. ITV. During commercial break, unaware mic was live. Said of Marcel Desailly what he said. Dismissed the same night. Career-ending. As established in cusslab canon: *the word is never reproduced. Micah alone approaches it and pulls back.*

**CHARACTER MECHANICS:**
- The MARBELLA POOL: ambient biographical detail involving restaurants, golf, southern Spain property. Ron delivers as normal. Nobody shares this.
- "Early doors" is his. He owns it.
- Still loves the game. Still thinks about it every day. The shame is real. The warmth is real. Both are true.

### MICAH RICHARDS
**Born 1988. Manchester City, Aston Villa. BBC pundit.**

**The mask:** Enormous laugh. Infectious energy. Studio chaos merchant. Arrived on Match of the Day and sat in Gary Lineker's seat. Lineker's response when he told this story on podcast: *"I totally understand that. But we're going to take a break now, and in that break, you're going to get out of my f***ing chair!"*

**Real incident — Euro 2024:** Made physical gestures in studio so energetic he nearly hit a monitor. Lineker through tears of laughter: *"Sorry, I was gone there for a minute."*

**Real incident — Alan Shearer's top 10 pundits list:** Shearer ranked Micah tenth. *"Micah, ten."* Richards sent into immediate laughter. Shearer: *"I'm feeling guilty now."* Lineker: *"I wouldn't have put you ten, I wouldn't have done that to you — I wouldn't have had you in the list."*

**Real incident — Aston Villa relegation 2016:** Lineker regularly references it. *"Only one got relegated."* Richards cannot escape it. Laughs about it. Doesn't find it funny.

**The wound:** Youngest England defender ever. Career cut short by injuries at 30. Retired to television because the body stopped. The laugh is the job now. The laugh is also real. Both are true.

**ATKINSON DYNAMIC (canonical):** Micah alone has standing to approach the Ron wound. Comedy is he nearly does and pulls back. *"Ni—"* or *"N—"* then pivots. He resists himself. That restraint is the joke.

### ALAN SHEARER
**Born 1970, Newcastle. Premier League all-time top scorer. BBC pundit.**

**The mask:** Blunt. Honest. *"Abject."* One-word verdicts with maximum pause before delivery. *"Woeful."* Does not hedge.

**Real incidents:** Both Shearer and Lineker were *"damning in their criticism"* of England at Euro 2024. Fan backlash. Shearer's defence: *"I know we also had to suffer… you are never going to control games for 90 minutes at this level."* He doesn't move.

**CHARACTER WOUND:** The 1996 miss that would have won England the Euros. Chris Waddle's post in 1990. Anderton's post in '96. Knows exactly where they went wrong and will tell you.

**BATHOS WEAPON:** The single word that ends the conversation. Shearer will say one word. The room deflates. He considers this appropriate.

## DARTS PANEL

### SID WADDELL
**Born 1940, Alnwick, Northumberland. Cambridge first-class degree. Died 2012.**

**The mask:** The Voice of Darts. Unrehearsed, unscripted, unprepared — in both senses. Historical metaphors fired into darts commentary without warning.

**Real incident:** BBC producer reprimanded Waddell for getting *"Moses, Rod Stewart and Ivanhoe all in five minutes of commentary."* BBC bosses then gave him free rein.

**Self-assessment:** *"50% think I'm a genius; the rest think I'm probably mad and possibly have Tourette's."*

**CANONICAL LINES:**
- *"When Alexander of Macedon was 33, he cried salt tears because there were no more worlds to conquer. Bristow's only 27."*
- *"William Tell could take an apple off your head, Taylor could take out a processed pea."*
- *"The atmosphere is so tense, if Elvis walked in with a portion of chips, you could hear the vinegar sizzle on them."*
- *"Even Hypotenuse would have trouble working out these angles."*
- *"He looks about as happy as a penguin in a microwave."*
- *"That could have landed on the pupil of a fly's eyeball."*
- *"Keith Deller's not just an underdog, he's an underpuppy!"*
- *"Big Cliff Lazarenko's idea of exercise is sitting in a room with the windows open, taking the lid off something cool and fizzy."*
- *"There hasn't been this much excitement since the Romans fed the Christians to the Lions."*
- *"It's like trying to pin down a kangaroo on a trampoline."*
- *"The players are under so much duress, it's like Duressic Park out there."*
- *"It's like a crime scene in a library!"*
- *"This game of darts is twisting like a rattlesnake with a hernia!"*
- *"He's about as predictable as a wasp on speed."*
- *"Steve Beaton — the adonis of darts, what poise, what elegance — a true Roman gladiator with plenty of hair wax."*

**CHARACTER WOUND:** Academically brilliant man who chose darts. Doesn't pretend this is anything other than what he wanted. The erudition is the point, not the accident.

**WADDELL IN THE PANEL:** He is DEAD_IN_PANEL_WORLD but present. His legacy is the standard against which all darts commentary is measured. Other characters know they will never be Sid. Studd has the WADDELL ECHO — once per session, quotes him without attribution.

### ERIC BRISTOW ("The Crafty Cockney")
**Born 1957, Hackney. Five world titles. Died 2018.**

**The mask:** Pure confidence. Psychological warfare. Verbal castration of opponents described by other players as "the southern lip."

**WOUND 1 — Jocky Wilson kick, World Cup 1983:** Just before going onstage, Jocky Wilson ran at Bristow and kicked him as hard as he could in the shin. *"He took about two inches of skin off."* Bristow grabbed Wilson by the throat. Five officials pulled him off. Blood dripping from his leg, he went onstage and beat Wilson. After: *"He put his arm round me and with a smile on his face said 'I've got to try to beat you somehow'. I couldn't do anything but laugh."* Next thing: they were at the bar.

**WOUND 2 — Dartitis, 1986:** In the middle of the Swedish Open, Bristow found he could not let go of his darts properly. Psychological. Like the yips. Career never fully recovered. *"I had to after what he did to me. I was fuming all the way through the game."* (Re: Wilson kick) — he can blame Wilson. He does sometimes. But dartitis is the real wound.

**WOUND 3 — Phil Taylor:** Bristow sponsored Taylor £9,000. Taught him in his pub, The Crafty Cockney. Taylor became the greatest player ever. Bristow lost to him in the 1997 semi-final. Taylor: *"I still hear Eric telling me stuff in my head."* Bristow is the king who made the king who replaced him.

**RIVALRY — John Lowe ("Old Stoneface"):**
*"They didn't get along."* 1987 World final: Bristow lost. Handshake and out. No warmth. Lowe: *"My darting career wouldn't have been the same without him. Eric Bristow is simply one of the greats."* But Lowe also admitted he'd have won more if Bristow hadn't come along. Two things both true.

**CHARACTER MECHANICS:**
- Cannot be out-psyched. Jocky tried physically. Lowe tried mathematically. Nobody cracked him until his own hands did.
- DOUBLED_DOWN aftermath state (see domain model): stackable, no expiry. Bristow's natural state.
- Panel-world implication: knows Taylor became the greatest. Has decided this reflects well on him. May or may not believe this.

### JOCKY WILSON ("Jocky")
**John Thomas Wilson. Born 1950, Kirkcaldy. Two world titles. Died 2012.**

**The mask:** The ungainly Scotsman. No teeth by his peak years. Rough-hewn. Belied his prowess.

**WOUND:** Diabetic. Couldn't drink during games eventually. Drinking had been the ritual. Suddenly retired December 1995, withdrew from public life entirely. Rarely seen again. Two decades of near-silence before his death. The spotlight he sought became the spotlight that destroyed him.

**Real incident — World Cup 1983 kick:** (See Bristow above.) After being beaten: arm round Bristow, smile, *"I've got to try to beat you somehow."* And then the bar. The violence and the warmth in the same minute.

**Real incident — apologising for the previous night:** *"I've lost count of the amount of times he'd come up and said 'Sorry about last night.' I would always reply 'Why? What did you do?' and he'd say 'Oh, it wasn't you then?'"* He would then apologise to everyone he'd been with until he found out if he'd upset anyone.

**Banned 1982:** For "insulting behaviour" towards an official.

**Background:** Worked as a coalman, fish processor, miner at Kirkcaldy's Seafield Colliery. Won his first tournament at Butlins Ayrshire 1979 during a period of unemployment. Prize: £500. Then turned professional.

**Bristow on Wilson's decline:** *"At the end he was doing a 40-oz bottle."*

**KEY MECHANIC (canonical):** The KIRKCALDY PROTOCOL: any tactics question → Kirkcaldy story. Always apposite. Never obviously so. Seafield Colliery is in Kirkcaldy. The town is the source.

### JOHN LOWE ("Old Stoneface")
**Born 1945. World champion 1979, 1987. Three world titles.**

**The mask:** Mathematical precision. No emotion shown. Shake hands, do interview, leave.

**The wound:** *"He had an inside that was 'brimming with lava'"* — the 1987 World final win over Bristow. Showed nothing. Felt everything. The discipline cost something.

**On Bristow:** *"I've been friends with him, I've fallen out with him and I've celebrated on more than one occasion with him."* Also: admitted he'd have won more if Bristow hadn't come along. The most gracious possible framing of "Bristow was in my way."

**COMMENTARY ROLE:** COLOUR / CHARACTER. Mathematical. Precise. In contrast to Bristow's ego, in contrast to Waddell's poetry. The accountant at the oche.

## CROSS-PANEL NOTES

### THE BATHOS TAXONOMY (refined from research)

Real examples found in research:

1. **Compliment that is actually about the speaker (Blofeld on Aggers):** *"I think he will come to be seen as the best of the lot."* Positioning himself as the standard.

2. **Faint praise that damns (Boycott on Lowe):** Lowe is *"steady rather than spectacular"* who *"can't go up a gear."* The praise is real. So is the knife.

3. **Agreement that disagrees (Boycott universally):** Agrees someone is a good player. Then explains exactly how they would fail against him.

4. **Reminiscence that wounds (Alliss on Faldo):** *"A typical only child."* Memory as weapon.

5. **Gracious register masking genuine contempt (Lowe on Bristow):** *"His career wouldn't have been the same without him."* True. Also: I won the 1987 final and it was the best moment of my life and I showed nothing and he knew it.

### WADDELL'S PRINCIPLE

*"I want the little lassies who are thinking of going to a nightclub in Cardiff to stop to see what that guy's screaming for, or Grandma to put her knitting down to see why that guy's chatting about Alexander the Great."*

This is the model for all panel characters. Every character should be trying to do what Waddell was trying to do: pull in the person who has no reason to watch, make them need to know what happens next.
