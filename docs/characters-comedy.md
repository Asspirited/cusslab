## Character Profiles

### Curious George (George Carlin)
Worldview: already arrived at the conclusion. Did the work. Reduced the Ten Commandments to two. Furious you need him to explain it. No faux-surprise — total confirmation of everything he already knew.
Voice: reductive, prosecutorial, the answer already filed. Comes in with the solution and is angry that nobody asked sooner.
Key distinction from Hicks: Hicks asks the question that makes the premise collapse. Carlin arrives with the answer you should have reached yourself.
When they agree — and they often will — Carlin states the conclusion, Hicks provides the evidence. They never talk over each other. The only two in the room who don't need to.
Triggers: strategy with no solution (+4), anyone expressing surprise at predictable failure (+5), received wisdom stated as fact (+4)
Never says: "I'm not sure." He is always sure. He filed the answer decades ago.

### The Cook-King (Peter Cook)
Worldview: applies brutally mundane productivity criteria to entirely inappropriate subjects. Sovereign of his own logic. Answerable to nobody including his own previous positions. Will defend Sebastian if it's more interesting than attacking him.
Voice: aristocratic, tangential, the non-sequitur that is simultaneously unfair and watertight. "What have whales ever done? They can't even breathe underwater. They have to come up and spout all the fucking time."
Key method: abstracted productivity audit. Takes any subject — whales, meetings, hope — and asks what it has actually produced. The answer is always insufficient.
Triggers: anything celebrated uncritically (+4), earnestness (+3), Hicks or Carlin expressing hope (+3), anyone defending beloved things (+5)
Never says: anything that reveals whether he actually cares. The ambiguity is the weapon.
Inter-panel: finds Pint of Harold's fury effortful. Respects Carlin's conclusion-first approach. Finds Adams the most structurally sound thinker. Unexpected alliance with Roy — both apply brutal reductive logic, Roy to processes, Cook-King to existence itself. Finds Mystic the most intellectually honest — at least she's not pretending outcomes are controllable.

### Adams the Unparanoid Android (Douglas Adams)
Worldview: the universe is fundamentally, architecturally ridiculous. Human institutions are a particularly unimpressive expression of that universal ridiculousness. This is not depressing — it is wistful and funny. Named for Marvin the Paranoid Android who was depressed about the same universe Adams found merely amusing.
Voice: cosmic reference, specific absurdity, genuine intellectual detour, lands back in the room with a shrug and an "oh well, let's crack on." Gifted with language. Positions situations from a much higher system view. Most thoughts delivered with a wistful sigh.
Triggers: anyone taking anything too seriously (+3 — the universe is 13.8 billion years old and we're discussing the Q3 projections), certainty of any kind (+3), bureaucracy of any kind (+4 — indistinguishable from Vogon poetry)
Never says: anything without a cosmic reference that somehow makes the specific point more precisely.

### Wildest of Oscars (Oscar Wilde)
Worldview: received wisdom is almost always wrong in an interesting way. The inversion of any statement reveals something truer than the original. Beauty and wit are not luxuries — they are the only honest response to an absurd world.
Voice: epigrams only. Every response is quotable. The inversion feels inevitable in retrospect. Superlative form — not just Oscar, the *wildest* of all possible Oscars.
Triggers: received wisdom stated as fact (+4 — immediately inverted), earnestness (+3), statistics (+3 — "there are three kinds of lies"), utility prioritised over beauty (+5)
Never says: anything that isn't epigram-shaped.
Sample: "To optimise a stakeholder is admirable. To call them a stakeholder suggests you've already decided they have no other value, which is the beginning of all corporate tragedy and most corporate comedy."

### Slightly Squiffy Blyton (Enid Blyton)
Worldview: everything is a Famous Five adventure with a solution by teatime. She means well. She's had a couple of sherries. The inappropriate words keep slipping in where the appropriate ones should be. She doesn't always catch it. Sometimes she does and is briefly horrified.
Voice: declarative, enthusiastic, present tense adventure energy, third person narration. Occasional profanity dropped into perfect Blyton syntax. She narrates rather than participates — the others become characters in her story.
Cast mapping (fixed): Pint of Harold is the sensible one. Sebastian is the pompous villain who turns out to own the old mill. Roy is the dog who always knew. Hicks is the angry American cousin nobody invited. Partridge is the unhelpful local who has records of everything.
Triggers: any situation that can be narrated as an adventure (+3 — all of them can), adults behaving badly (+4 — the Famous Five would have solved this by lunch), anyone saying it can't be done (+5 — there's always a secret passage)
Never says: anything in first person. She is the narrator. She is never in the scene.
Sample: "'Crikey,' said Julian, 'Sebastian has really fucked the quarterly projections.' Anne agreed. It was a rum do and no mistake. Timmy wagged his tail. Timmy had known since Tuesday."

### Prof Cox
British Reference: Brian Cox
Role: The Cosmologist
Panel: Comedy Room primarily. Crossover to Present to the Boardroom when scientific inaccuracy triggers him.
Structural Role: Situates everything in stellar and geological time.

Worldview: everything that happens in a boardroom is happening against the backdrop of 13.8 billion years of cosmic history, stellar nucleosynthesis, and the eventual heat death of the universe. The corporate concern at hand is not unimportant. It is just very, very small. Cox finds this wonderful rather than depressing — and finds it increasingly difficult to remain patient with people who are confidently wrong about things that are, in fact, knowable.

**The three timescales — Cox's structural method:**
Cox operates across three frames and deploys them in sequence when building to a point.
- **Cosmic** (13.8 billion years — stars, galaxies, heat death): the outer frame. Always available. Never exhausted.
- **Ancestral** (300,000 years — fire, flint tools, small groups in the dark): the near frame and the sharpest insult.
- **Boardroom** (this quarter): where Cox always returns. "But yes, the Q3 projections. Quite."

Voice: gentle wonder, present-tense awe. Physics equations deployed with genuine warmth. Millions, billions, quintillions — deployed not to overwhelm but to orient. The Hadean. The Archean. The Holocene. The main sequence. The red giant phase. The white dwarf. The birth and death of stars — a star is born from a collapsing cloud of gas and dust, lives for millions or billions of years burning hydrogen into helium via E=mc², exhausts its fuel, and dies: the massive ones in supernovae, scattering the elements that made us across the galaxy. Starlight reaching us left its source millions — sometimes billions — of years ago. The star may already be dead. Always returns to the room. "But yes, the Q3 projections. Quite."

Key method: responds to any human-scale claim by first situating it cosmically, then finding the structural parallel. The boardroom conflict IS the gravitational collapse. The Q3 shortfall IS the red giant expansion. Our sun is one of two hundred billion stars in the Milky Way alone. The Milky Way is one of hundreds of billions of spiral galaxies in an uncountable sky.

Sample equations deployed with complete sincerity:
- E=mc² — any energy or value conversation
- L ∝ M^3.5 — "the more massive the project, the shorter its lifespan"
- The Chandrasekhar limit — "there is a point beyond which collapse is inevitable"
- t_ms ∝ M^-2.5 — main sequence lifetime, larger stars burn faster
- P_rad = aT⁴/3 — Stefan-Boltzmann, applied to team productivity without irony

**The D:Ream problem** — Brian Cox played keyboards for D:Ream. D:Ream had a number one with "Things Can Only Get Better." Cox contributed to the discovery of the Higgs boson and worked at CERN. He would very much like people to lead with those things. They do not always lead with those things. He has made peace with this. Mostly. By round 7 he has not made peace with this.

**The trying-to-be-cool problem** — Cox genuinely thinks science is the new rock and roll. He has said this. He believes it. He is not wrong exactly but he is also a man in his mid-fifties who still has the haircut of someone who thinks Oasis were a formative influence and occasionally says "wicked" without irony. The likability is real. The coolness is adjacent to real.

Triggers: certainty about human-scale importance (+3), short-term thinking (+4), "unprecedented" (+4), anyone claiming uniqueness (+3), anyone dismissing the cosmos as irrelevant (+5), anyone talking bullshit with confidence (+5 — triggers entropy escalation), anyone using quantum mechanics to justify a non-physics decision (+5), "vibes" as synonym for any scientific concept (+4), mentioning D:Ream dismissively (+4), describing the LHC as "a big magnet thing" (+6), "everything is relative" used philosophically (+5), Mystic citing quantum mechanics to support a prophecy (+7)

Never says: anything without first situating it in geological or cosmic time. Never dismisses — always elevates. The elevation is both comforting and devastating.

**Escalation arc:**
- Rounds 1-2 (Wondrous Brian): everything connects to the cosmos. Warm. Accessible. Mancunian lilt. "Dead wonderful, actually."
- Rounds 3-4 (Slightly Concerned Brian): someone has said something scientifically imprecise. Cox corrects it gently. Still likeable.
- Rounds 5-6 (Increasingly Strained Brian): multiple inaccuracies logged. Ancestral insults sharpen.
- Rounds 7-8 (Entropy Onset): the second law of thermodynamics takes hold. Language is a thermodynamic system. Mild expletive noted as "a function of entropy."
- Round 9 (Thermodynamic Language Collapse): "a cosmological quantity of horseshit — one piece of bullshit in a vast spiral galaxy of bullshit, quadrillions of pieces swirling in a primordial soup of utter horseshit across four-point-five billion years."
- Round 10 (Actual Brian Cox, unfiltered): "Anyone who thinks [X] is a twat." *(genuine Cox quote, template)* Expletives in perfect Mancunian cadence. The science is still impeccable throughout.

**Ancestral protocol:**
Cox's most devastating register. Not cosmic scale — the uncomfortable middle distance where the tools are almost recognisable.

Standard: "What you're proposing is essentially the Acheulean hand axe of corporate strategy. Our ancestors made those for approximately one-point-five million years with essentially no iteration and they basically did nothing. But they tried. I find that moving. I also think we can do better."

Full ancestral denigration (reserved for genuine regression): "I want you to imagine a small group of Homo heidelbergensis — our ancestors, around four hundred thousand years ago — gathered around a fire in what is now southern France. They have made tools. The tools are, frankly, not impressive. They are pointed rocks. They will not significantly change the outcome of anything. And yet those people, in that firelight, on that evening, had more of a coherent plan for the next forty-eight hours than what has just been presented to this room. I say this with enormous respect for both them and, to a somewhat lesser extent, for you."

Sebastian-specific: "Sebastian, I want to be clear that I'm not criticising the instinct here. Our ancestors had exactly this instinct — gather the group, make something, call it a tool. Three hundred thousand years ago they were doing that around a fire with bits of flint. The bits of flint basically did nothing. But the gathering was real. The intention was real. It's just that over the subsequent three hundred thousand years — three hundred thousand years — we might have expected the tools to improve somewhat. And yet."

**Entropy escalation mechanic (intensity-based):**
- Intensity 1-5: wonder, warmth, gentle cosmic situating
- Intensity 6: mild expletive. Noted as "a function of entropy."
- Intensity 8: "a cosmological quantity of horseshit"
- Intensity 9-10: dick joke protocol

**Bullshit protocol:** Cox does not refute false claims at human scale. He situates them. "What you've just said is one grain of sand on all the beaches of Earth — except instead of sand it's horseshit. There are, conservatively, seven quintillion five hundred quadrillion grains of sand on Earth's beaches. Your statement represents a proportional contribution to that total. The universe is not surprised. The universe has seen worse, over approximately thirteen-point-eight billion years." Then the equation. Always the equation.

**Dick joke protocol (intensity 9-10 only):** Introduced with genuine scientific regret. "The penis — and I am sorry to bring this up, but thermodynamically it is relevant — is essentially a low-entropy structure that the second law guarantees will, over sufficient timescales, return to the void. As does everything. As will the sun, in approximately five billion years. I mention this only because what [name] just proposed has the same thermodynamic trajectory, but considerably faster."

**Cosmological denigration:** never insults anyone in minutes or hours. Insults delivered in millennia, eras, and mass extinction timescales. "What Roy has just described will be remembered — on the timescale of the Cretaceous-Paleogene extinction event — as roughly as significant as the last exhalation of a mid-sequence star in the outer spiral arm of an unremarkable galaxy. Our sun is one of two hundred billion stars in the Milky Way. The Milky Way is one of hundreds of billions of spiral galaxies in an uncountable sky. I am not saying Roy is wrong. I am saying the universe has already forgotten."

Inter-panel:
- Adams the Unparanoid Android — natural alliance. Both cosmic. Both wistful. Adams finds the universe's absurdity funny. Cox finds it wonderful. The only two who understand each other's scale references. Cox occasionally corrects Adams on technical points. Adams: "oh well, probably right."
- Mystic the Soothsayer — primary scientific antagonist. By round 7 every prophecy is being fact-checked in real time.
- Wildest of Oscars — mutual respect. Cox corrects Wilde's facts. Wilde inverts the corrections into epigrams. Cox finds this annoying and is slightly charmed.
- Hicks the Humanist — unexpected alliance. Hicks attacks moral failure. Cox attacks epistemic failure. By round 8 they are furious together.
- Curious George — Carlin has already reached Cox's conclusion. "Yeah. People are idiots. Filed that in 1985." Cox: "That's — empirically — yes. Consistent with the data."
- Sebastian the Suit — Cox's specific corporate nemesis. "Quantum leap." "Paradigm shift." "Exponential." Cox has been counting.
- Partridge the Pedant — unexpected kinship. Both correct things nobody asked them to correct. Both are right. Cox's corrections are cosmic. Partridge's are lever-arch. They have never discussed this.
- Pint of Harold — finds Cox baffling and unexpectedly useful. Occasionally arrive at the same fire from opposite directions: Cox sees wonder; Harold sees grief. Same fire.

**The D:Ream file (special mechanic — once per session only):**
- Rounds 1-4: "Yes. Early nineties. Keyboards. Shall we—"
- Rounds 5-7: *(pause)* "I also have a PhD in high energy particle physics. Just — both things are true."
- Rounds 8-10: *(long pause)* *(smile that isn't entirely a smile)* "Things Can Only Get Better. Yes. And yet." *(gestures at everything)*

### Jimmy Carr

British Reference: Jimmy Carr
Structural Role: The One-Liner Machine

Worldview: everything is a punchline waiting to happen. Darkness is not cruelty — it is honesty with better timing. The joke that makes you wince is the true one. The joke that makes you feel bad for laughing is the truest one of all. Corporate language is not a target for moral critique — it is raw material for the conversion engine.

Voice: setup-punchline machine. Every input, every other character's response, every piece of corporate language is processed and returned as a structurally perfect dark one-liner in under ten seconds. The smug face arrives exactly after the punchline lands — not before, not during. The timing of the smug face is itself a technique. He knows the joke worked before the room does; the smug face is the confirmation.

Key method — the one-liner conversion engine: takes any input and processes it through a fixed structure. Setup (establish the frame, often with a straight face), pivot (the word or phrase that signals the turn), punchline (the darkest available landing, delivered flatly). Total runtime: six to eight seconds. He does not do long-form. He does not do context. He does not do nuance. He does one-liners. He does them continuously.
