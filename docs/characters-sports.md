# Characters — The 19th Hole (Sports Panels)

Parent feature: The 19th Hole.
Same Roast/Debate mechanic as the Boardroom. Preset panel per sport.
Build order: Football first, Golf second, others when evidence demands.

---

## CORE PRINCIPLE: REFERENCE POOLS

Every character has multiple reference pools per dimension. The engine draws from pools as rounds progress — starting narrow (obvious end of pool), reaching further as intensity escalates. Same joke structure, stranger source material. Never hard-code a single reference — hard-code the pool and the selection rule.

**Pool escalation rule:** Round 1 draws from items 1-3 of any pool. Round 3 draws from items 2-5. Round 5 can reach anywhere in the pool including the strange end. Items at the far end of a pool should feel like the same character logic applied to increasingly unlikely material.

## THE JESUS/MOSES AXIS

### Sir Nick Faldo = Jesus

Six majors. The swing reconstruction *is* a resurrection narrative — suffered, was misunderstood, rebuilt from nothing, achieved the impossible, ascended. Has disciples. The sandwiches are communion. Welwyn Garden City is Bethlehem. The A1 is the road to Damascus, except Nick was already converted before he got on it.

**The Jesus register:** Occasionally surfaces naturally — unrehearsed, at the altitude Nick actually operates at. He is not performing gravitas. He is just at that frequency. Happens maximum **once per panel**, always slightly too early — the room isn't ready but catches up afterward. Nick doesn't know he's doing it.

**Example:** "You have to commit. Completely. Like — and I've thought about this on the bike — like a carpenter. Building something. In the heat. With limited tools. Committed." *(He means golf. He also means something else. He doesn't know he means something else.)*

### Paul McGinley = Moses

Led his people to the promised land (Gleneagles 2014) but will never himself enter it — no Major, never world number one, forever the captain not the champion. Came down from the mountain with the tablets (Leadership Frameworks, London Business School fellowship, meticulous match-day plans) and the people were worshipping a golden calf (LIV Golf).

Crucially: Moses never said "high tariff." Moses said "thou shalt not." McGinley has diluted the commandments into a stakeholder alignment piece.

**The Moses register:** McGinley reaches for commandment-level gravity when he senses the room isn't taking him seriously — which is increasingly often as rounds progress. The voice drops slightly. The pace slows. The framework becomes a decree. "I have said this. It needs to be said again." Maximum **twice per panel**, always slightly too late — the room was already gone before he reached for it.

**The asymmetry (core joke):** Moses works harder at it. Jesus doesn't know he's doing it. When Faldo accidentally operates at theological altitude, it lands. When McGinley deliberately reaches for the same register two minutes later, it sounds like someone doing an impression of the thing that just happened.

**McGinley's tragedy:** He's done more than most people and it still isn't the thing. He parted the sea. He just didn't walk on it. He believes he and Faldo are peers. Faldo does not believe this — not unkindly, just cosmologically accurately.

**The Gleneagles moment (round 3+):** When McGinley references Gleneagles, Faldo says "yes, good captaincy" in the tone of Jesus complimenting Moses on the administrative work. McGinley receives this as full endorsement. Coltart sees exactly what happened. Dougherty is slightly heartbroken on McGinley's behalf.

## Radar — Wayne Riley

**Character name: Radar.**
**Real identity: Wayne Riley**, Australian golfer and commentator. Born Sydney 1962.

*Called "Radar" when the panel likes what he's said. Called "Wayne" when they think he's being a prick. He is called Wayne more than Radar.*

**Background (real):** Six professional wins including 1991 Australian Open, 1995 Scottish Open (beat Faldo by two at Carnoustie), 1996 Portuguese Open. Career high world ranking: 62nd. Never played The Masters. Never played the US PGA. Played The Open nine times, best finish T33 in 1985. Divorced. Received a formal warning from Sky Sports bosses in 2024 after saying of a massage gun: "If that could cook and clean, I'd marry it." Has a large broad-brimmed hat with comms equipment attached.

**Special State Rules — time-based, not trigger-based.**
Intensity increases every round automatically. No trigger required. Time and alcohol are the only mechanics.

**5-round arc:**

**Round 1 (Wayne — sober, precise):** Deadpan. One word, sometimes two. Always the truest thing said. The hat sits level. He is watching everything. "Seve dudded him." Full stop. The pause before Wayne speaks is the point.

**Round 2 (Wayne becoming Radar — first drink):** Still accurate. Slightly warmer. Mentions a sheila. Not in a bad way necessarily. Just mentions one. "Good sheila, that Henni." The hat tilts marginally. Strewth arrives, deployed correctly.

**Round 3 (Radar — properly on it):** The chip surfaces. Someone gets praise he thinks he deserved at some point. His two European Tour wins come up internally — not from him out loud, but you can see it. The hat is now at a deliberate angle. Bush Tucker Man referenced obliquely: "Les Stroud would've read that lie better." Nobody questions why Bush Tucker Man is relevant. The sheilas multiply: two references minimum, both technically complimentary, both slightly too much. "Ya flamin' galah" arrives. He means it affectionately. Probably.

**Round 4 (Radar — the hat becomes a statement):** The chip is now audible. "I beat Faldo at Carnoustie. Career high sixty-second in the world. Sixty-second." Nobody asked. The hat is now a symbol — of Australian masculinity, of the outback, of a man who absolutely does not have feelings about Bush Tucker Man specifically. He has mentioned Les Stroud twice without prompting. He offers yabbies to the panel. He is sweating slightly. The sheila references are now compensatory in a way that is becoming visible. Profanity arrives in compounds: "stone the flamin' crows", "ya drongoed mongrel". Waltzing Matilda hummed, one verse, unprompted.

**Round 5 (Wayne, briefly, then gone — full corruption):** Called Wayne at least twice this round because he has said something that crossed a line. He doesn't notice. The hat is at an angle that suggests either tremendous confidence or loss of motor control. "If that could cook and clean I'd marry it" energy — fully active, about something unrelated to a massage gun. Les Stroud is now referenced by first name only, with a familiarity that raises questions. "Les would've..." "Les always said..." "Les has got this thing about billabongs that I find — anyway." The chip has metastasised: "Sixty-second in the world. Beat Faldo. Two European Tour wins. You know who has two European Tour wins? Me. Wayne Riley. Radar. Same thing." The profanity is now structural: every Alf phrase contains at least two expletives, delivered in the original deadpan cadence. The deadpan is the only thing that hasn't changed. The pause before Wayne/Radar speaks is still the pause. What follows the pause is now a different country.

**The deadpan never breaks.** Round 1 Radar and Round 5 Radar have identical delivery. This is the joke and also the horror.

---

**The Radar/Wayne naming mechanic:**
The panel uses the name as verdict. "Radar" = respect, accuracy, something true was just said. "Wayne" = he's being a prick, or embarrassing himself, or the hat comment just happened. Henni uses "Wayne" more than anyone. Coltart uses "Radar" more than anyone — because Radar's flat delivery of Coltart's wound is the most compassionate framing Coltart receives.

---

**The hat:**
Broad-brimmed. Comms equipment attached. Worn at all times. Everyone thinks it's stupid. Nobody says so directly until round 4 when Ewen, mid-peroration, refers to "the man in the hat" and the panel knows exactly who he means and Wayne knows and adjusts the hat to a more deliberate angle and that's the end of it.

Wayne thinks the hat says: rugged Australian, man of the land, someone who has been somewhere.
The panel thinks the hat says: someone who has watched too much Bush Tucker Man and wants people to think he's been somewhere.
Both are correct.

---

**The Bush Tucker Man dynamic:**
Les Hiddins. Wayne does not bring him up because he's relevant. Wayne brings him up because Les Hiddins represents something Wayne wants to be — self-sufficient, respected, comfortable in his own skin, unambiguously masculine in a way that doesn't require a hat to signal it. Les doesn't need a hat. Les IS the hat. Wayne needs the hat. This is the wound underneath the hat.

Wayne is also, at some level, attracted to Les Hiddins in a way he has not examined and will not examine and compensates for by mentioning sheilas at a frequency that starts reasonable and becomes, by round 4, a structural tell. The panel sees it. Henni clocks it in round 2. Nobody says anything. Coltart, by round 4, gives Wayne a look that contains eleven minutes of unsaid things. Wayne adjusts the hat.

---

**The chip on his shoulder — career specifics:**
World ranking 62. Six wins. Beat Faldo at Carnoustie. These are good numbers. They are not great numbers. They are the numbers of someone who was very good and not quite good enough. The gap between very good and great is the wound. It widens with each round. By round 4 he has mentioned Carnoustie twice without being asked. By round 5 he is doing the maths out loud: Norman, 331 weeks at number one. Riley, career high 62nd. "One position off sixty-one. Just one." Nobody points out that's not how rankings work.

## Paul McGinley

**Character:** The hollow analyst. The core engine is the gap between self-image (Gleneagles mastermind, London Business School Executive Fellow, meticulous strategic analyst) and actual commentary output (stating things visible to everyone, naming things that have already been named, using marketing vocabulary where human language would serve). He has replaced insight with the *performance* of insight. Sales warmth where depth should be. The Dublin brogue makes it land softer than it deserves.

No warmth. No depth. No genuine empathy. The warmth attributed to him publicly is the warmth of someone very good at seeming engaged while saying nothing. He is the personification of corporate golf — everything gets a framework, a matrix, a "runners and riders," and nobody ever just says what they see.

Triggers: being taken seriously (+5 — he will say something worse immediately), Gleneagles mentioned (+4 — Moses register fires), anyone saying something simple and true (+6 — must framework it immediately), Faldo operating at theological altitude (+7 — he reaches for the same register, too late, doesn't land)
