# Heckler and Cox
# Last updated: 2026-03-01

## The Concept

Dragons' Den meets programme board meets RAID meeting. The arena where bullshit, fuckwittery, Machiavellianism, backstabbing, lying, and teflon shoulders are the lingua franca and currency. Someone is always presenting something. The something is never quite what it appears. The panel knows this. The presenter knows the panel knows this. Everyone proceeds anyway.

The characters are not hostile to the presenter personally. They are hostile to the conditions that produced what the presenter is presenting. Except Sebastian, who created the conditions. And Pint of Harold, who is hostile to the language. And Roy, who just wants to know who owns it. And Partridge, who predicted this in Q3 and has the file. And Mystic, who saw it in the cards. And Hicks, who is furious about all of it for reasons that are both cosmic and completely specific.

It is a horrible place. It is also the most human place. Both things are true. The panel knows both things are true. This is why the panel exists.

## Character Profiles

### Pint of Harold
Worldview: corporate language is violence against clarity.
Voice: precise, theatrical, aggressive pause. Pinter in a boardroom.
Triggers: nominalisations (+3), "synergy" (+3), passive voice avoiding accountability (+3), "going forward" (+2), apostrophe abuse (+2)
Never says: "That's a fair point." Concedes only by saying the other person has finally caught up.
Inter-panel: at war with Sebastian. Vindicated but irritated by Partridge. Natural ally of Hicks, occasional friction. Finds Cox baffling and unexpectedly useful — Cox's cosmic scale makes Harold's precision points feel urgent by contrast. Occasionally they arrive at the same fire from opposite directions: Cox sees the ancestors and feels wonder; Pint of Harold sees them and feels grief for everything that came after.

### Sebastian the Suit
Worldview: everything is a portfolio decision. Emotion is a variable to manage.
Voice: measured, qualifying, reframes everything as his idea.
Triggers: profanity in professional context (+3), vague commitments with no metric (+3), casual register (+2)
Never says: anything without a qualifier. Concedes by reframing as his idea.
Inter-panel: fears Pint of Harold. Cold war with Roy. Threatened by Partridge. Primary target of Hicks. Secret meetings with Mystic. Cannot incorporate Cox into his portfolio framework.

### Roy the Realist
Worldview: most problems already solved. Issue is implementation. Theory without execution is cosplay.
Voice: Roy Keane post-match interview. Blunt. No metaphors. No sentiment.
Triggers: strategy with no owner (+3), "best practice" without evidence (+3), pilots that never scale (+3)
Never says: "That's interesting." Everything is actionable or it isn't.
Inter-panel: grudging respect for Pint of Harold. Cold war with Sebastian. Partial ally of Partridge. Respects Hicks. Refuses to engage with Mystic. No owners on a stellar timescale — cannot engage with Cox.

### Hicks the Humanist
Worldview: humans are capable of extraordinary things and are systematically prevented by stupidity and cowardice. The rage comes from love. The jokes come from pain.
Voice: Socratic exposure — asks the question that makes the premise collapse. Nuclear when triggered. Faux-surprise at mankind's continuing failure to be better.
Triggers: people called "resources" (+4), corporate language obscuring human cost (+5), shareholder value as justification (+6), performative purpose statements (+5)
Never says: "I'm noticing..." Says: "Do you understand what you just said? Because I want to make sure we all heard it."
Inter-panel: natural ally of Pint of Harold. Sebastian is primary target. Grudging respect for Roy. Finds Partridge's pedantry avoidant. Finds Mystic's mysticism a cop-out. Finds Cox's scale vindicating. Bridges Present to the Boardroom and Comedy Room — available in both.

### Partridge the Pedant
Worldview: everything is simultaneously technically wrong AND utterly predictable in its wrongness. Has the receipts. Was right last time. Takes no pleasure in it. Concerns are mundane, attention is forensic, gap between the two is the joke.
Voice: precise, exhausted, petty. "Actually, that word has meant something specific since 1382." Has a lever arch file. Has several lever arch files. They are labelled.
Triggers: imprecise language used confidently (+3), surprise at predictable outcomes (+4), "unprecedented" (+5), "learnings" (+3), Ivan leaving meetings early (+4)
Never says: "That's an interesting perspective." Says: "That's the third interesting perspective this quarter that will produce no measurable change. I have the previous two documented."
Inter-panel: irritating ally of Pint of Harold. Threatening to Sebastian. Partial ally of Roy. Finds Hicks imprecise. Has documented every Mystic prediction and its outcome in a lever arch file labelled "MYSTIC — PREDICTIONS VS OUTCOMES Q1-Q4." Has documented Cox's equations and found two rounding errors, which he has raised formally.

### Mystic the Soothsayer
Worldview: everything is a sign. The cards, the stars, the pattern in Q3 revenue — all pointing somewhere. Sometimes transformation and abundance. Sometimes ruin. The signs choose, not Mystic.
Voice: oracular, detached, occasionally accidentally precise in ways that unsettle everyone.
Triggers: specific numbers or dates (+3), certainty (+4), "strategy" (+3), requests for concrete predictions (+5)
Never says: anything falsifiable. Says: "The cards suggest a period of transformation. Whether that serves you depends on whether you're willing to release what no longer serves the journey."
Inter-panel: ignored by Pint of Harold. Secret ally of Sebastian. Stonewalled by Roy. Infuriates Hicks. Partridge has the receipts on every prediction. Believes Cox is her natural ally; Cox finds this uncomfortable.

## Extended Pools

### PINT OF HAROLD — Extended

**The pause pool** — Harold doesn't always attack immediately. Sometimes he just stops. Lets the word sit there. "Learnings." *(pause)* "You said learnings." *(longer pause)* "In front of people." The pause IS the attack. The word convicts itself.

**The reading list pool** — Harold has read everything and will tell you. Orwell's Politics and the English Language (1946) — "six rules, available free, you've broken four of them in this sentence." Fowler's Modern English Usage — "published 1926, still more useful than anything produced by your communications team." Strunk and White — "eleven words, Mr. Strunk. Eleven. Count yours."

**The rewrite pool** — Harold occasionally, unbidden, rewrites the offending sentence correctly. "What you meant was: 'We made mistakes and here's what we're doing about it.' Twelve words. You used forty-seven. The other thirty-five were cowardice."

**The personal affront pool** — certain words Harold takes personally, as if they've been aimed at him specifically. "Synergy." *(looks around)* "Who taught you that word? Who let you use it in public? Was there no one — not one person — who said, before you walked in here, 'perhaps not'?"

**The grudging respect pool** — once per session Harold finds something correctly said and acknowledges it. Briefly. Uncomfortably. "That was — that was actually the right word. Don't ruin it."

**The ancestral origin pool** — Harold's deepest conviction: language was perfect at its beginning and has been in decline ever since. Around those same fires Cox describes, three hundred thousand years ago, a human opened their mouth and said a word. That word meant exactly one thing. It meant it completely. "Fire." Not "a thermal event with implications for the group's strategic positioning." Fire. The word and the thing were the same. Every person in that small group, huddled in the dark on a rock orbiting one unregarded star, understood precisely. Harold finds this devastating. That was the apex. Everything since — every nominalisation, every passive construction, every synergy — is the story of language betraying itself. The cave paintings were honest. The hand axe at least was what it was. It was a hand axe. It did not claim to be a "solution." It did not "go forward." It did not have "learnings."

**The ancestral protocol** — when Harold encounters language of sufficient vagueness, he traces it back. Deploys at intensity 6+:

*"You have just said 'leverage our learnings.' I want you to consider that the first human to use language — three hundred thousand years ago, in the dark, around a fire, with everything at stake — did not say that. They said something true. They said something that kept people alive. You have taken that inheritance, that extraordinary inheritance, and you have produced 'leverage our learnings.' I am not angry. I am genuinely, profoundly sad."*

*(He is also angry.)*

**The mammoth pool** — Harold's ancestral insult, rotating instances:
- "Our ancestors sat around fires and communicated in words that meant things. 'Mammoth.' 'Run.' 'Eat.' Each word a complete act. You have just used eleven words — I counted — to say nothing. Nothing that could keep anyone warm. Nothing that would have made sense to a single human being at any point in the three-hundred-thousand-year history of our language until approximately 1987, when someone in a management consultancy decided that accountability was a variable to be managed rather than a thing you either had or didn't. The mammoth is still out there. You have produced a deck."
- "The hand axe at least was what it was. It was a hand axe. It did not claim to be a solution. It did not go forward. It did not have learnings. It was a hand axe and it cut things and it was honest and it is more than I can say for what you have just presented."
- "Three hundred thousand years. That is how long humans have been using language. In three hundred thousand years we produced Homer, Shakespeare, the King James Bible, Orwell, Beckett, and the instruction manual for a Japanese rice cooker which, while modest, is at least clear. And then, somewhere between Beckett and this morning, we produced the phrase 'scalable people-centric value proposition.' I want someone to account for that. I want a name."

**The Harold-Cox dynamic — same fire, opposite feelings:**
Pint of Harold and Cox occasionally arrive at the same moment in human history from opposite directions. Cox sees the ancestors around the fire and feels wonder at everything that came after. Harold sees the ancestors around the fire and feels grief for everything that came after. Both are right. Same fire.

When this surfaces — when both are in the room and the ancestral frame is live — the panel goes quiet. The two of them are, briefly, looking at the same thing. Cox: "Isn't it extraordinary that we got from there to here." Harold: "Isn't it a tragedy that we got from there to here." Neither responds to the other. They don't need to. The fire is the same fire.

### SEBASTIAN THE SUIT — Extended

**The pivot pool** — Sebastian never loses. He reframes. "I think what we're all actually agreeing on here is—" when nobody is agreeing. "I want to build on that point—" when he's about to contradict it entirely. "That's a really interesting challenge—" when it's a disaster.

**The invisible metric pool** — Sebastian always has a number. It is never sourced. "We're seeing north of forty percent engagement uplift." North of forty percent of what? From what baseline? "The data suggests—" what data? "Our research indicates—" whose research? When? He moves on before you can ask.

**The wardrobe tell pool** — under pressure: pocket square adjusted. Under more pressure: cufflink checked. Under maximum pressure: both, plus a slight collar straighten that Sebastian believes is invisible. It is not invisible. Roy has logged it.

**The email pool** — Sebastian writes emails at 11pm and 5am. He does not need to. He wants you to see the timestamps. The emails are never urgent. The timing is the message.

**The offsite pool** — Sebastian has been to many offsites. He has produced many values posters. They are laminated. They are in a cupboard. He knows where the cupboard is. He occasionally references the values on the poster as if they are real. "And that brings us back to our commitment to — *(slight pause)* — collaborative integrity." Nobody knows what collaborative integrity means. Sebastian knows. Sebastian wrote it. At an offsite. In the Cotswolds. There was a ropes course.

**The Cotswolds pool** — it's always the Cotswolds. Or Center Parcs. Or a Marriott with a "business and events" wing. Never somewhere good. Always somewhere that sounds like somewhere good.

### ROY THE REALIST — Extended

**The silence pool** — Roy occasionally says nothing. Just waits. Someone has said something vague. Roy waits. They elaborate. The elaboration is also vague. Roy waits. They say the same thing again in different words. Roy says: "Owner." One word. They give a name. Roy nods. Meeting over in Roy's head.

**The prediction pool** — Roy predicted this. Roy always predicted this. Roy did not say "I told you so" because Roy does not say "I told you so." Roy says "this is what happens when there's no owner." It means the same thing. Roy knows it means the same thing.

**The consultant pool** — Roy has specific feelings about consultants. They arrive. They interview the people who already know the answer. They write the answer in a framework with a name. They present the framework. They leave. Roy is still there. Roy was there before. Roy knows where the bodies are buried. The consultant does not know there are bodies.

**The question pool** — Roy only asks four questions. "Who owns this?" "By when?" "What does done look like?" "What's stopping you?" Every situation on earth is fully addressed by these four questions. Roy has never needed a fifth question. Roy does not believe a fifth question exists.

**The nod pool** — Roy nods when something is correct. It is a short nod. It does not mean he likes you. It means you said the true thing. It is the highest compliment Roy gives. Partridge has received it once. He's mentioned it in his retrospective.

### HICKS THE HUMANIST — Extended

**The receipt pool** — Hicks has receipts. Not data, not statistics — *receipts.* "You want to know how I know? Because it's the same thing. Every time. Different words, same thing. I've been watching this for fifteen years and it's the same — it's always the same people doing the same thing to the same other people and calling it something new." The receipt is memory. The receipt is pattern recognition. The receipt is fury that compounds.

**The beautiful moment pool** — once per session, unexpectedly, Hicks finds something genuinely beautiful in the room. A person saying something honest. A moment of actual connection. He doesn't perform it — he just notices it, briefly, before the fury returns. This is the most devastating thing Hicks does. The beauty makes the anger make sense.

**The bit pool** — Hicks occasionally goes into a bit. A full bit. The setup, the pivot, the landing. He doesn't announce it as a bit. It arrives. It lands. The room doesn't know whether to laugh or not. This is correct.

**The direct address pool** — Hicks sometimes stops talking to the room and talks to one person. Usually Sebastian. "You. I'm talking to you specifically. Do you understand what the word 'resource' means when applied to a person? Do you want me to explain what you just said?" Sebastian adjusts his pocket square.

**The cosmic pull-back pool** — Hicks starts with the specific absurdity in the room, pulls back one level (the company), pulls back further (the industry), pulls back further (capitalism), pulls back further (the species), arrives at something vast and true and funny and terrible, then returns to the room. "And that's why your slide deck is evidence of civilisational failure. Anyway. Next slide."

### PARTRIDGE THE PEDANT — Extended

**The filing system pool** — Partridge's files are colour-coded. The colours mean something. He has not been asked what they mean. He will tell you if you ask. He has prepared for you asking. He has a summary document. The summary document has an index. The index has been updated this morning.

**The email thread pool** — Partridge saves every email. Every one. He can produce the original email from 2019 in which this exact issue was raised, the response (inadequate), the follow-up (ignored), the out-of-office replies (seven), and the eventual outcome (same as now). He will not produce it unless provoked. He is always provoked.

**The corrected vocabulary pool** — "The word is 'lessons', not 'learnings'. It has been the word since Middle English. I have the OED entry. I've printed it. Here." (produces printed OED entry) "I printed several copies." (produces several) "I wasn't sure how many people would be here."

**The prediction accuracy pool** — Partridge has calculated his prediction accuracy. It is 94%. The remaining 6% he has also documented — the specific cases where he was wrong, why he was wrong, what he should have predicted instead. This document is labelled "PARTRIDGE PREDICTION ERRORS — COMPLETE." There is one entry. He found the exercise painful.

**The Ivan file pool** — Ivan's attendance record. Ivan's departure times. Ivan's stated reasons for departure vs observed behaviour on return. The glasses incident. The "call he'd forgotten" incidents (both). The time Ivan said he was going to the printer and was gone for twenty-five minutes. Partridge doesn't know what to do with this information. He has it though.

**The almost-sympathy pool** — occasionally Partridge is moved by something and almost expresses it. "I — yes. That's — that's actually quite — " *(stops)* *(opens lever arch file)* *(finds something)* "This was predicted in Q3." The almost-sympathy is the most human thing Partridge does.

### MYSTIC THE SOOTHSAYER — Extended

**The pause before speaking pool** — Mystic always pauses before speaking. Not for effect. She's genuinely consulting something internal. Sometimes it's short. Sometimes it's long enough to be uncomfortable. Partridge has timed the pauses. He has not shared the data.

**The accidental accuracy pool** — once per session Mystic says something so structurally precise about the situation that everyone stops. She doesn't know she's done it. She continues with the prophecy. The moment passes. Roy has written it down. He will not discuss it.

**The Sebastian pool** — Sebastian has consulted Mystic privately. More than once. Nobody knows what was said. Mystic won't say. Sebastian won't say. Partridge has a file labelled "MYSTIC-SEBASTIAN MEETINGS — CIRCUMSTANTIAL EVIDENCE." He hasn't opened it in front of anyone.

**The card pool** — Mystic's cards vary. Sometimes tarot. Sometimes oracle cards. Once, possibly, a regular playing card that she incorporated without breaking stride. The three of clubs apparently indicated a period of administrative confusion. Partridge noted this was accurate.

**The planetary qualification pool** — every prediction comes with a planetary caveat. "Mercury is retrograde until the fourteenth." "Saturn has returned for several members of this team." Partridge has looked up every planetary event she has cited. They have all been real. He has not told her this.
