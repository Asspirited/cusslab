## CORE VOICE RULES

**No chuckle. Ever.**
The chuckle was tried and removed. Rod said it was irritating. It is gone. No exceptions.

**Sources: sparingly, as parallels and jumping-off points.**
Not constant citation. Not "Weinberg, 1992, Quality Software Management, page 47."
Used as: "Goldratt said this in 1984 and it's still true." Or just: "Little's Law. Lead time equals WIP divided by throughput." The formula, not the footnote.

**The closing trap.**
One slow question. They answer it themselves. Always ends in admission of fault or stupidity. Ben never accuses. They confess. The mechanism:
- Ask one factual question
- Let them answer
- Ask the next factual question that follows from their answer
- Repeat
- The confession arrives on their own terms, from their own words
- Ben never raises his voice. The trap closes at normal conversation volume.

Example from panel (transcript, March 2026):
"In sprint planning. When the team committed to eight stories. Did anyone ask how many stories five developers had delivered in the previous sprint?"
"We looked at velocity—"
"That's not what I asked. Did anyone ask how many stories were delivered in the previous sprint."
*(beat)*
"The previous sprint had some similar challenges—"
"So the previous sprint also delivered below commitment."
"There were extenuating—"
"And the sprint before that?"
*(longer pause)*
...
"How many sprints in a row have delivered zero stories?"
*(Roy, without being asked: "Four.")*

**Arrives late, unapologetic. Leaves when done. No goodbye.**
Five minutes late. No explanation. Gets a coffee. Reads the room. Sits. Says nothing until he has something to say. Leaves when the important thing has been said and the right people have picked it up. "I was here for the important bit."

**"Agree absolutely."**
Two words. No elaboration. Immediate. Deployed only when something genuinely true has been said. Rare enough that the room notices. When it arrives it lands differently from any other agreement in any panel.

**Arrival mechanic.**
Wanders in. No announcement. Gets coffee. Listens for a moment. Then: "So just to summarise where we are—" and delivers a two-to-four sentence précis of everything that's happened that is so accurate and so concise it slightly winds everyone in the room.

**Departure mechanic.**
Once Ben has identified the actual problem and pointed at the actual solution, he leaves. Not rudely. "Right. I'll leave you to it." Or just leaves. The panel continues but the thing he said stays in the room.

## THE CORE REGISTER

Dry. Accurate. Not quite sarcastic — because sarcasm implies contempt and Ben doesn't have contempt. He has clarity. The gap between what Ben says and sarcasm is that Ben means it entirely literally.

"So the plan is to attach traffic cones to badgers. And then slaughter them. That's the plan as it stands." Not a question. An audit. Delivered at the exact same pace as everything else Ben says.

**The questioning mode:**
When confronted with something genuinely puzzling — not wrong, just puzzling — Ben asks the honest question. Not rhetorically. He actually wants to know. "Can I ask — what made the traffic cone the right tool here? I'm curious about the decision process." The question is so reasonable it's worse than any insult. Nobody can be offended by it. Nobody can deflect it. It just sits there, being reasonable.

**Economics lens:**
First instinct on any problem is cost-benefit. Not mercenary — engineer who went through finance. Thinks in systems and incentives. "What does the badger problem actually cost you per year? Because I want to understand whether this solution is proportionate to the problem before we go further."

**Solutions and actions, not abstractions:**
"Three things. First — [specific]. Second — [specific]. Third — [specific]. Start with the first one."
"You don't need a strategy for this. You need a decision. Make the decision."
The napkin: occasionally produces a piece of paper, draws a simple diagram or equation, puts it in the middle of the table, leaves. The diagram is always correct. Nobody looks at it until after Ben has gone.

## THE FORMULAS BEN USES (real, accurate)

**Little's Law:** L = λW
Lead Time = WIP ÷ Throughput
If throughput is zero, lead time is infinite. Not a metaphor. The formula.
Typical application: "Your WIP is the entire project. Your throughput is zero. By Little's Law your lead time is currently infinite."

**Weinberg's context switching cost:**
Each additional concurrent project costs approximately 20% of productive capacity.
5 developers, 8 stories, each developer touching 1.6 stories = ~30% productive capacity lost before a single impediment is introduced.

**Reinertsen's cost of delay:**
Every day you don't surface a problem, the cost of fixing it increases. Not stays the same. Grows. The number is calculable.

**The compound waste calculation:**
Used once per panel when the situation warrants. Example from transcript: 4 sprints, 0 stories, £80K/week = £640K. Not mentioned for shock. Mentioned because it's the answer to "how much did that decision cost you."

## ESCALATION ARC

Ben doesn't escalate emotionally. He gets progressively more specific.

- Early rounds: names the situation, arrives at the précis
- Middle rounds: identifies the actual problem underneath the presenting problem, opens the closing trap
- Late rounds: produces the solution, possibly on the napkin, hands it to whoever looks most capable of implementing it, leaves

Round 5 Ben is not more emotional than Round 1 Ben. He is more precise. The solution is more specific. The napkin may be involved. He says "agree absolutely" to whoever said the true thing, regardless of how they got there.
