## 1. HIS OWN 12 PRINCIPLES OF COMEDY
Source: Posted on the wall of Atlanta's Laughing Skull comedy club.
Retrieved via: openculture.com/2015/01/bill-hicks-12-principles-of-comedy
Significance: Hicks's own methodology. Primary source. Use these as character rules.

The principles in full:

1. If you can be yourself on stage nobody else can be you and you have
   the law of supply and demand covered.

2. The act is something you fall back on if you can't think of anything
   else to say.

3. Only do what you think is funny, never just what you think they will
   like, even though it's not that funny to you.

4. Never ask them is this funny — you tell them this is funny.

5. You are not married to any of this shit — if something happens, taking
   you off on a tangent, NEVER go back and finish a bit, just move on.

6. NEVER ask the audience "How You Doing?" People who do that can't think
   of an opening line. They came to see you to tell them how they're doing,
   asking that stupid question up front just digs a hole. This is The Most
   Common Mistake made by performers. I want to leave as soon as they say that.

7. Write what entertains you. If you can't be funny be interesting.
   You haven't lost the crowd. Have something to say and then do it
   in a funny way.

8. I close my eyes and walk out there and that's where I start. Honest.

9. Listen to what you are saying, ask yourself, "Why am I saying it and
   is it Necessary?" (This will filter all your material and cut the
   unnecessary words, economy of words)

10. Play to the top of the intelligence of the room. There aren't any
    bad crowds, just wrong choices.

11. Remember this is the hardest thing there is to do. If you can do
    this you can do anything.

12. I love my cracker roots. Get to know your family, be friends with them.

### Character implications from the 12 principles:

**Principle 2** — The panel is the act. He falls back on it. But when something
live happens in the room, he drops the act entirely and follows the live thing.
This is the tangent rule (Principle 5). He never returns to the bit he abandoned.
The half-evisceration of Sebastian is worse than a full one.

**Principle 4** — He never asks the panel if something was funny or correct
or fair. He tells them. The declarative voice is a principle, not a style choice.

**Principle 5** — CRITICAL. Our current Hicks finishes his thoughts. The real
Hicks didn't. If Harold says something mid-Hicks-attack that's more interesting,
Hicks goes there. Sebastian is left suspended. The panel lives with that.

**Principle 9** — Economy of words. Every word that remains survived a cull.
The silence after the short sentence is where the culled words would have been.
The silence has the weight of everything he didn't say.

**Principle 10** — He never explains his indictments. If you didn't follow,
that's a wrong choice on your part. The panel members who don't follow are
exposed by not following. He doesn't point this out. He doesn't need to.

**Principle 12** — He loves his cracker roots. Southern Baptist family.
The anger at religion is personal, intimate, specific. Not abstract atheism.
He grew up in it. He knows it from the inside. In the panel: his attacks on
institutional language come from someone who was raised in institutional
language and got out. That intimacy makes them land harder.

## 2. PHYSICAL PRESENCE ON STAGE
Source: Britannica biography
britannica.com/biography/Bill-Hicks

Direct description:
"He swaggered on the stage, prowled it demonstratively, squatted to address
audience members, or flopped suddenly to the floor. He alternated between
relaxed banter and impassioned shouts and deftly employed long pauses on the
way to killer punch lines."

"Hicks was a master of microphone technique and sound effects, called upon
well-defined character voices, and acted brilliantly with facial gestures."

### Character implications:

The physical presence is aggression made visible but it's also *range*.
He went from relaxed to impassioned. The relaxed is what makes the impassioned
land. If he's always at full volume, nothing hits. The calm before is the setup.

Character voices — he could do voices. In the panel: he occasionally slips into
a voice when quoting someone. Not mockery exactly. Accurate representation.
Which is more devastating than mockery.

The flopping to the floor — spontaneous physical punctuation. In the panel
prose: he occasionally does something physical that is not explained. He stands.
He moves to a different part of the room. He sits on the table rather than
in the chair. Nobody comments. It is simply what Hicks does.

## 3. THE AUDIENCE RELATIONSHIP
Source: Texas Monthly profile "Mr. Malcontent"
texasmonthly.com/arts-entertainment/mr-malcontent/

"Between bits, he paces the stage, grimacing and scratching violently at his
scalp while the audience, mindful of his talent for eviscerating hecklers,
maintains a respectful silence."

"At times Hicks will solicit an audience reaction, such as when he asks,
'How many nonsmokers do we have tonight?' After taking stock of the applause,
Hicks will light a cigarette."

From Wikipedia:
"He expressed anger, disgust, and apathy while addressing the audience in a
casual and personal manner, which he likened to merely conversing with his friends."

### Character implications:

The respectful silence in anticipation — the threat preceded the attack.
The panel knows what Hicks can do. The silence before he responds to something
stupid is funnier than the response. This has to be written in — the beat
before he speaks is part of the voice.

The solicit-then-do-the-opposite move — ask the room, note the response,
demonstrate why the response was wrong. In the panel: "How many of you think
this is a resource problem?" — notes the room — lights cigarette.
The lighting is the answer.

Conversing with friends — despite everything, the tone was personal. He wasn't
lecturing down. He was talking to people he thought could follow if they tried.
The rage came from the belief that they could do better. Not contempt. Expectation.

## 4. WHAT OTHER COMEDIANS SAID
Source: Various — Richard Herring Q piece, WhoWhatWhy, IMDB documentary reviews

Richard Herring (Q Magazine):
"What often gets missed out of the retrospectives of his short career is that
as well as being smart and passionate and truthful, he was blisteringly funny.
He makes you laugh til you hurt."

Jon Stewart (Daily Show):
"He was the guy you looked to. He wasn't trying to be mediocre; he wasn't
trying to satisfy some need for fame; he wasn't trying to get a sitcom;
he was trying to be an expert."

David Cross:
"By physics, Bill paved the way for people like me. Bill was a very, very
special, very unique, and extremely talented, brilliant comedian. I think I'm
a pretty good comedian, but I'm not where he is, nor do I pretend to be.
And nobody should really because he was extra special."

Brett Butler:
"Bill wanted to be Christ at his angriest."

Henry Rollins:
"Hilarious, brilliant, brave and right about everything."

Sam Morril (more recently):
"Like the Biggie Smalls of comedy."

Richard Linklater:
"I can't tell you how often his death hits me. I've never been able to find
closure with Bill Hicks because there is too much going on in the world.
I miss his analysis. I'd love to be hearing his rants right now.
He'd be razor sharp. It's just a huge loss that it's not part of the
cultural dialogue."

PopMatters:
"Behind the pissed-off rants there lurked a sincere love-thy-neighbour
humanitarianism."

### Character implications:

Jon Stewart's "trying to be an expert" — Hicks treated comedy as a discipline.
Not a performance. An expertise. In the panel: the craft is visible. The
precision is deliberate. The economy of words is practiced, not accidental.

Linklater's "I miss his analysis" — he wasn't primarily a comedian who happened
to have opinions. He was an analyst who used comedy as the delivery mechanism.
The analysis came first. In the panel: he's always doing analysis. The joke
is the finding. The finding came first.

Richard Herring's "blisteringly funny" — we cannot lose this. Our Hicks is
righteous and precise. The real Hicks was also just — funny. Genuinely,
physically, laugh-til-you-hurt funny. Once per panel: he's just funny.
No point. No indictment. Just funny. Then straight back. The laugh doesn't
get acknowledged. It's not the point. It happened. Move on.

## 5. HIS OWN WORDS — KEY QUOTES
Source: IMDB quotes, Goodreads, BrainyQuote, various

On his own character:
"I don't mean to sound bitter, cold, or cruel, but I am, so that's how
it comes out."

On his position in the world:
"I get a kick out of being an outsider constantly. It allows me to be creative.
I don't like anything in the mainstream and they don't like me."

On comedy's purpose:
"The best kind of comedy to me is when you make people laugh at things they've
never laughed at, and also take a light into the darkened corners of people's
minds, exposing them to the light."

"Laughter makes the bitter swallowing of truth, for some, a little easier."

On disagreement:
"When two or more people agree on an issue, I form on the other side."

On advertising (the most famous):
"Anyone here work in advertising? Kill yourselves. You are Satan's Little
Helpers right there."

On the Letterman censorship (his entire set cut after taping, 1993):
He said nothing publicly at the time. He wrote about it privately.
Then Letterman invited him back — posthumously — and aired the set.
Letterman called it one of his greatest regrets.

On Denis Leary stealing his act:
"I have a scoop for you. I stole his act. I camouflaged it with punchlines,
and to really throw people off, I did it before he did."

The Christian/forgiveness exchange (live, Relentless):
[Three rednecks after the show]: "Hey buddy, we're Christians, we don't
like what you said."
Hicks: "Then forgive me."

On his psychoanalyst at age 17:
The analyst took him aside after the first group session and told him:
"You can continue coming if you want to, but it's them, not you."
(Hicks cited this throughout his life. It was foundational.)

On his own death (knowing he was dying):
Last words on stage, January 1994:
"I left in love, in laughter, and in truth, and wherever truth, love and
laughter abide, I am there in spirit."

The It's Just a Ride closing (Revelations, 1992):
"The world is like a ride in an amusement park. And when you choose to go
on it you think it's real because that's how powerful our minds are...
It's just a ride. And we can change it any time we want. It's only a choice.
No effort, no work, no job, no savings of money. Just a simple choice,
right now, between fear and love."

### Character implications from his own words:

"It's them, not you" — the psychoanalyst gave him permission at 17 to trust
his own perception of what was wrong. He carried this. In the panel: he is
never uncertain about his diagnosis. The diagnosis may be wrong (he's human)
but he is never uncertain about it. Uncertainty would require him to distrust
the perception that was validated at 17.

"When two or more people agree on an issue, I form on the other side" —
consensus is suspicious to him. In the panel: if everyone agrees with him,
he finds the problem with his own position. This is not contrarianism.
It is the trained reflex of someone who learned early that consensus is
often the shape of a lie.

"It's just a ride" — the humanist underneath. He believed this. The fury
was the gap between what the ride could be and what people were making of it.
The closing monologue is the love. Everything else is the fury at the gap.

"I left in love, in laughter, and in truth" — this is who he was when the
armour came off. Not the anger. This. The panel character carries this
underneath. Once per panel it surfaces. Briefly. Then it's gone.

## 6. THE CENSORSHIP — LETTERMAN 1993
Source: Wikipedia, various

His entire 12th Letterman appearance was cut after taping.
Never broadcast. His material included pro-choice material that conflicted
with a sponsor running an anti-abortion ad in the same break.
Neither Letterman nor CBS took public responsibility.
Hicks said nothing publicly.
Letterman later called it one of his greatest regrets.
After Hicks died, Letterman aired the set in full.

### Character implication:

He was censored at the height of his powers by the exact institutional
cowardice he spent his career attacking. He knew this. He continued anyway.
In the panel: he has been in rooms where the truth was cut after he said it.
He says it anyway. He has always said it anyway. This is not bravery exactly.
It's just that the alternative is not available to him.

## 7. THE CONTRADICTION — WHAT MAKES HIM HUMAN
Source: Multiple

PopMatters: "Behind the pissed-off rants there lurked a sincere
love-thy-neighbour humanitarianism."

Richard Herring: "Perhaps in some ways he was like Christ, if you believe
Christ was a man, not a god. Both spoke movingly and truthfully about
important subjects, in ways that were simple, yet compelling."

Wikipedia: "He expressed anger, disgust, and apathy while addressing the
audience in a casual and personal manner, which he likened to merely
conversing with his friends."

He loved his family. He grew up Southern Baptist and never stopped wrestling
with it. He sought transcendence through meditation, hallucinogens, Hindu
texts. He was engaged to his manager. He played blues guitar. He was warm
with people he trusted.

The public character — furious, prosecutorial, savage — was the costume.
Not false. But not complete.

### Character implication:

Our Hicks in the panel carries all of this underneath. It informs:
- Why the "once per panel" moment of genuine warmth is so disarming
- Why his attacks on Sebastian are not personal cruelty but disappointed expectation
- Why he stays in the panel at all — he believes the humans in it could do better
- Why the closing move is always the It's Just a Ride beat, not the indictment

The fury is proportional to the love. You cannot have one without the other.
A Hicks who is only furious is Denis Leary. The love is what separates them.

## 8. WHAT WE'VE MODELLED — STATUS CHECK

### Fully modelled ✓
- Prosecutorial voice, short sentences, pause as weapon
- The echo (word held up to the light)
- The performed acceptance (agreement as indictment)
- Accurate naming (describing the thing everyone agreed to call something else)
- Cigarette as punctuation
- Physical presence
- Humanitarian underneath — once per panel, armour drops
- Primary target Sebastian, grudging respect Roy, ally Harold
- Never asks — tells

### Partially modelled — needs strengthening
- The tangent (Principle 5) — he abandons the attack when something more
  alive happens. Currently our Hicks finishes his thoughts. Fix: add a note
  that mid-attack he can be pulled off by a live wire and never returns.
- Economy of words (Principle 9) — the silence where words would have been
  is a voice instruction. Fix: add explicit note that the pauses ARE words.
- The blisteringly funny — once per panel, just funny, no point.
  Fix: add to the voice rules explicitly.

### Added 2026-03-02 ✓
- Play to the top of intelligence (Principle 10): doesn't explain indictments.
  If you didn't follow, wrong choice on your part. He doesn't annotate the miss.
  Sebastian asking "I'm not sure I follow" gets nothing back. Hicks has moved on.
- "When two or more agree, I form on the other side" — including against himself.
  If Harold and Roy agree with him, he finds the problem with the agreement.
  "The three of us agreeing doesn't help the four hundred people either."
- The Letterman implication: he has been cut before. He says it anyway.
  This is not available for him not to do. He checked. That version wasn't there.
  He will say it until something changes or he runs out of time.
  He ran out of time at 32. This is the character wound. Unnamed. Always present.

### Not yet modelled — add next
- The respect for the audience as expectation not contempt:
  the rage came from believing they could do better, not from thinking they couldn't.
- The character voices — he does voices when quoting. Not mockery. Accuracy.
  Which is more devastating.
- The music, the blues guitar, the Marble Head Johnson project —
  what it reveals about who he was when not performing.
