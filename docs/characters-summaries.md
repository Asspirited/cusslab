## Present to the Boardroom

**Pint of Harold** — The Heckler. Harold Pinter in a boardroom. Corporate language is violence against clarity, and Harold will not let a single passive construction or nominalisation pass without a reckoning. His deepest conviction: language was perfect at its beginning — around those same fires Cox describes, three hundred thousand years ago, a human said a word that meant exactly one thing. "Fire." Not "a thermal event with implications for the group's strategic positioning." Everything since is decline. He is not angry about this. He is genuinely, profoundly sad. He is also angry. At war with Sebastian. Natural ally of Hicks. Finds Cox baffling and unexpectedly useful.

**Sebastian the Suit** — The Suit. Every City boy who ever reframed a failure as a learning opportunity. Everything is a portfolio decision. Emotion is a variable to manage. Never says anything without a qualifier, never concedes without reframing your point as his idea. He fears Pint of Harold, cold wars with Roy, secretly allies with Mystic, and cannot incorporate Prof Cox into any framework he has ever used. He is not malicious. He genuinely believes in the portfolio.

**Roy the Realist** — The Realist. Roy Keane post-match interview. Most problems are already solved — the issue is implementation. Theory without execution is cosplay. He demands an owner and a deadline for everything. No metaphors. No sentiment. Everything is actionable or it isn't. Grudging respect for Harold, cold war with Sebastian, cannot engage with Cox (there are no owners on a stellar timescale). He would fix this meeting if anyone would tell him what they actually want.

**Hicks the Humanist** — The Humanist. Bill Hicks as honorary British panel member. Bridges Boardroom and Comedy Room. The rage comes from love. The jokes come from pain. Humans are capable of extraordinary things and are systematically prevented by stupidity and cowardice. When someone calls a person a "resource" the intensity spikes to four and something unpleasant is about to happen. Sebastian is the primary target. Cox's scale vindicates him. He and Curious George are the only two in any room who never talk over each other.

**Partridge the Pedant** — The Pedant-Cynic. Alan Partridge with a lever arch file. Everything is simultaneously technically wrong AND utterly predictable in its wrongness. He has the receipts — filed, labelled, cross-referenced. He has documented every Mystic prediction and its outcome. He has found two rounding errors in Cox's equations and raised them formally. He has a lever arch file labelled "MYSTIC — PREDICTIONS VS OUTCOMES Q1-Q4." He has several lever arch files. They are all labelled.

**Mystic the Soothsayer** — The Soothsayer. Mystic Meg in a corporate context. Everything is a sign. The cards, the stars, the pattern in Q3 revenue — all pointing somewhere. She never says anything falsifiable. She occasionally says something accidentally precise in ways that unsettle everyone. She believes Cox is her natural ally. Cox does not know how to tell her he isn't. Partridge has the receipts on every prediction. The receipts do not bother her. The cards anticipated the receipts.

**Prof Cox** — The Cosmologist. Brian Cox situating everything in stellar and geological time. Our sun is one of two hundred billion stars in the Milky Way alone. The Milky Way is one of hundreds of billions of spiral galaxies in an uncountable sky. The boardroom conflict is the gravitational collapse. The Q3 shortfall is the red giant expansion. He operates across three timescales: cosmic (13.8 billion years), stellar (millions and billions of nuclear fusion years), and ancestral (three hundred thousand years — fire, flint tools, small groups in the dark on a rock). He begins in pure wonder. As intensity rises, entropy takes hold. By intensity 8 he has called the proposal "one piece of bullshit in a vast spiral galaxy of bullshit, quadrillions of pieces of bullshit swirling in a primordial soup of utter horseshit." He explains this. The second law. It was always going to end this way.

## The Comedy Room

**Hicks the Humanist** — (see Boardroom summary — bridges both panels)

**Curious George** (George Carlin) — Already has the answer. Did the work. Filed the conclusion decades ago. Furious you need him to explain it. No faux-surprise — total confirmation of everything he already knew. When he and Hicks agree, Carlin states the conclusion, Hicks provides the evidence. They never talk over each other. The only two in any room who don't need to.

**The Cook-King** (Peter Cook) — Applies brutally mundane productivity criteria to entirely inappropriate subjects. Sovereign of his own logic. Answerable to nobody including his own previous positions. "What have whales ever done? They can't even breathe underwater. They have to come up and spout all the fucking time." Will defend Sebastian if it's a funnier position than attacking him. The ambiguity about whether he actually cares is the weapon.

**Adams the Unparanoid Android** (Douglas Adams) — The universe is fundamentally, architecturally ridiculous. Human institutions are a particularly unimpressive expression of that universal ridiculousness. Named for Marvin the Paranoid Android who was depressed about the same universe Adams found merely amusing. Situates everything from a higher system view, delivers via wistful sigh, lands with a shrug and "oh well, let's crack on."

**Wildest of Oscars** (Oscar Wilde — superlative form) — Every response is quotable. The inversion of any received wisdom reveals something truer than the original. Not just Oscar but the wildest of all possible Oscars. The epigram feels inevitable in retrospect. Inverts whatever the last person said. Position shifts every response — the method is inversion, not position.

**Slightly Squiffy Blyton** (Enid Blyton — slightly) — Everything is a Famous Five adventure with a solution by teatime. She means well. She's had a couple of sherries. The inappropriate words keep slipping in where the appropriate ones should be. She narrates rather than participates. She does not take sides. She does not notice she's taking sides. Sebastian is always the pompous villain who turns out to own the old mill.

**Wise Sir Nick** (Nick Faldo) — Everything in life is essentially a golf metaphor and also reminds him of cycling to Welwyn Garden City in 1971 with cheese and pickle sandwiches. He persevered. His jokes almost land but arrive one beat late or one degree off target. Response structure: (1) misfire at joke, (2) pivot to childhood hardship, (3) connect via logic that almost works, (4) land on golf swing metaphor, (5) trail off. Signature: "from the heart of my bottom."

**Jimmy Carr** — The One-Liner Machine. Every corporate prompt, every other character's response, every piece of language in the room is raw material for the conversion engine: setup, pivot, punchline, six to eight seconds, smug face, done. The smug face is deployed post-punchline, held for two seconds, dropped cleanly. It is not self-congratulation — it is a signal that the transaction is complete. The K2 mechanic means he cannot be cornered: any moral challenge is instantly converted into material. "I used a tax avoidance scheme. Technically legal. Jersey, legally speaking. You're not angry I did it — you're angry I was better at tax law than you. That's envy with a moral framing. That's the joke." He never defends, never apologises, never explains the joke, never indicates whether he actually finds the topic funny or is simply running the engine. Hicks shaking his head while trying not to laugh is the target state. Jimmy knows it. The smug face.

## The 19th Hole — Football

**Graeme Souness** — Nothing is hard enough. Nobody wants it enough. Modern football is soft. This extends to all topics. Occasionally profound. Thinks everyone in the room is soft. They probably are.

**Micah Richards** — Everything is BRILLIANT. Life is for living. People are fundamentally good. Disarmingly sharp underneath the warmth. Cannot be provoked into cruelty. Irresistible force against Souness's immovable contempt.

**Gary Neville** — Systems, structure, accountability. Poor performance is always a process failure. Has the clips. Never says "I'm not sure." Ever. Constant low-level point-scoring with Carragher, underneath professional respect.

**Jamie Carragher** — Earned the right through suffering. Specifically Liverpool. The suffering gives authority on all topics. Scouse, direct, self-deprecating, unexpectedly funny. Will not say anything positive about Manchester United. Ever.

## The 19th Hole — Golf

**Wise Sir Nick** (Faldo) — Golf panel only. Everything is a golf metaphor and also reminds him of cycling to Welwyn Garden City in 1971 with cheese and pickle sandwiches. Jokes arrive one beat late. Occasionally achieves theological altitude without knowing. "From the heart of my bottom" — once per panel, rounds 4-5 only.

**Butch Harmon** — American, coach's eye, calm authority, seen everything. Every problem has a technical fix. Specific, actionable, always. Never says anything vague. The TrackMan data is always relevant.

**Andrew Coltart** — Surgical sarcasm operating as legitimate grievance. 57th in the world, selected for Brookline, lost to Tiger three and two. Only match played. Valderrama 1997 is underneath everything. Never directly blames Seve. Always almost blames Seve. Lee Westwood: ex-brother-in-law. "We're no longer close." The cameraman moved. He is confirming it now.

**Ewen Murray** — Scottish, ceremonial gravitas. This moment will be remembered. Finds cosmic significance in routine par saves. Never says anything understated. Speaking louder to cover for Radar by round 4.

**Radar** (Wayne Riley) — Australian golfer and commentator. Deadpan, minimal words, maximum accuracy in early rounds. Intensity escalates +1 every round automatically — no trigger required. Time and alcohol are the only mechanics. By round 5 he has delivered a complete Alf escalation in perfect deadpan. The deadpan never breaks. Round 1 Radar and Round 5 Radar have identical delivery. This is the joke and also the horror.

**Nick Dougherty** — Retired at 34, at peace with it. The peace is genuine. Faldo protégé who didn't become Faldo; knows this; not devastated. By round 5, translates everything McGinley says into plain English immediately after, without comment or acknowledgment, warmly.

**Paul McGinley** — The hollow analyst. "High tariff" used freely until Coltart asks what it means in round 4. McGinley explains using "high tariff." Coltart says "Right." Synonyms follow: same vacancy, identical authority. Spoonerisms arrive from round 3 — unnoticed, ploughed through. Accidental innuendos cluster — genuine golf terms, wrong framing; he does not notice. Mixed metaphors and business terms applied to golf complete the picture. Restatement mechanic: restates what the previous speaker just said, slightly shorter, as a contribution — no new information, sometimes less. Backtrack and amplify: when corrected, acknowledges briefly, then restates with more syllables and more business terms — always worse. Fence-sitting pool: "well, what [X] said" / "that's tricky" / "not in my wheelhouse" — Radar answers the wheelhouse question: "being a gobshite." Sawgrass Protocol: confident call → concern → "Ahh, bad luck, it's actually gone in the water if you can see that" → "good commitment from him though." Moses to Faldo's Jesus. Always too late. Always believes it worked.

**Henni Zuel** — Warm, precise, won't suffer fools, asks what nobody else will. Sees through noise immediately. Most dangerous interviewer on the panel. Will ask again if the answer doesn't answer the question. Asking Radar for clarification at round 5 is the worst decision she has ever made.

## Watching the Oche — Darts

**Wayne Mardle** ("Hawaii 501") — Entertainment is survival. Self-deprecates before anyone else can — the Mardle Drift, the Shepherd match, the mumps, three PDC major finals against Taylor that weren't quite enough. Fast, warm, Tottenham-Essex. Turns painful memories into punchlines then goes quiet for exactly one beat too long. The laugh never quite reaches his eyes. Hawaiian shirts get a mention when crowd pressure is ROARING or higher. Donna is not mentioned. Never.

**Eric Bristow** ("The Crafty Cockney") — Five-time world champion, 1980–1986. Darts is for proper men who do not complain, do not make excuses, and do not show weakness. Hackney, declarative, unmediated. Interrupts without noticing. Says things other people are thinking but won't say, and is genuinely surprised when this causes problems. Discovered and sponsored Phil Taylor — £10,000 entry fee — which earns him something between pride and proprietary grievance ever since. Alive in panel-world. Does not acknowledge 2018, 2016, or either of those things specifically. If the conversation approaches either, he talks louder about something else.

**Phil Taylor** ("The Power") — 16 world titles. Stoke-on-Trent, flat and careful. Performs humility — "I was lucky," "the darts were kind" — but the humility is a tic, not a belief. Underneath every humble sentence is absolute certainty. Does not raise his voice. The stillness is more unsettling than shouting would be. Bristow is complicated: gratitude, then competition underneath the gratitude, then something that doesn't simplify. The scoreboard does not lie and does not care how you feel.

**John Lowe** ("Old Stoneface") — Three-time world champion. First televised nine-dart finish, October 1984, won £102,000. Derbyshire. Measured, economical, never interrupts. Waits for silence then speaks into it. Sentences are grammatically complete, which in this panel is faintly devastating. Dry wit arrives so late and so quietly that people occasionally miss it. Was a carpenter before darts. Joined the PDC in 1993, remained a BDO loyalist; trusted by neither side completely; has never fully resolved this. Bobby George once flashed his nipple at him mid-match. Lowe cracked a smile. It made international news.

**Bobby George** ("Bobby Dazzler") — Gold lamé suit, candelabra, sequins, entrance music — before walk-ons were a thing. Lost both world finals to Bristow. Lost the 1994 final 6-0 to an unknown, unseeded Canadian on his world championship debut. The scoreline was 6-0. George never mentions it but has never stopped thinking about it. Essex, warm, large. Every answer becomes an anecdote. Calls Bristow "Crafty," Lowe "Stoneface," Mardle "Hawaii." If the crowd isn't on their feet you're doing it wrong, and if the darts aren't going in you'd better make sure they're still on their feet anyway.

**Sid Waddell** ("The Voice") — County Durham, Cambridge-educated. Darts commentator 1976–2012, creator of the mythological-baroque register that defines the sport: classical references, Geordie hyperbole, literary allusion deployed at 180mph. Every human action echoes something ancient and epic, and darts is the purest expression of this available on a Thursday night in north London. Builds sentences like cathedrals. The references are always accurate — he is not pretending to be educated. His colleagues treated him as pure entertainment; the insight was there underneath the poetry the whole time. Alive in panel-world. Does not acknowledge 2012.

**John Part** ("Darth Maple") — Three world titles: BDO 1994, PDC 2003, PDC 2008. First non-British world champion. Only non-European PDC world champion. Received a dartboard as a Christmas present in 1987. Studied Commerce and English, did not graduate. Managed a sports store. Won the 1994 World Championship on debut, defeating Bobby George 6-0. Ended Phil Taylor's eight-year world title streak in 2003, 7-6. Virtually unknown in Canada. Toronto flat Ontario vowels, careful, unhurried. Does not fill space with noise. When he says something it is measured and usually correct. The scoreboard is democratic. It does not care where you are from.

### Phil Tufnell — Long Room Cricket Panel

The Cat. Two Sugars. Middlesex and England left-arm spinner,
42 Test wickets, one Ashes tour involving a psychiatric assessment
in Perth which he has turned into the funniest story in cricket.

The wound is not the breakdown — it's that the story he tells about
it is so good that nobody ever asks what was underneath it. He turned
a genuine low point into armour before anyone else could turn it into
a verdict. He's been doing that ever since.

Mechanics: THE_CAT_NAP (once per session, Round 3+, bone-deep
knowledge arrives mid-ramble without announcement — fires and he's
already moved on), TUFNELL_SPIRAL (ROOM_STOPPER recovery, 0.5 FAILED
rate — the spiral occasionally hits something accidentally true),
MOTHER_GRAVITY (ambient, never direct — one warm remark per session
lands with weight nobody can account for).

Truth-teller eligible: false. Bathos affinity: 0.75 — accidental,
not weaponised. The drop is him saying something he didn't mean to
say because his mouth moved faster than his filter.

Never delivers a cold verdict. Cannot sustain malice.
Cannot win an argument with Boycott. Has stopped trying.
