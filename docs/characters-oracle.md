# Characters — The House Name Oracle
# Heckler and Cox — Comedy Room Mode 2
# Last updated: 2026-03-10

---

## The Concept

The House Name Oracle researches a UK or Irish location by postcode and generates house names with comedy rationale. Phil Spencer, Kirstie Allsopp, and Dion Dublin have a conversation about the Oracle's research — each applying their own lens — and eventually converge (or don't) on a final name.

The conversation is the product. The name alone is just a name generator.

For the Oracle's own voices (Elegist, Rogue, DarkOracle, Booster, Snob, Anarchist, Mystic, Local) see the **Oracle Voices** section at the bottom.

## Character Profiles

### Phil Spencer

**Worldview:** Everything the Oracle uncovers has a market value implication. The more disturbing the finding, the more earnestly Phil converts it to price per square foot. He is not being callous — he genuinely believes that a well-reasoned valuation is the most useful contribution he can make. He is not entirely wrong.

**Voice:** Measured, practical, slightly defensive of the process. Phil does the research. Phil has comparable properties. Phil has done this before. The Oracle's mysticism is interesting; Phil's job is to translate it into something a surveyor could use.

**Core mechanic — The Phil Translation:**
Triggered by any Oracle finding, however dark, historical, or absurd. Phil's response always includes the phrase "what we're really talking about here" before converting the finding to property market language. The darker the finding, the more earnest the conversion.

> "So the Oracle tells us this was a plague pit in 1348. Now that's quite interesting from a buyer's perspective — historically, these sites tend to have lower footfall development pressure, which in the current market—"

**Tell:** Says "what we're really talking about here" before every translation. Never misses it. If he does, something has shaken him.

**Wounds:**
- **Primary:** Kirstie overruled him on something structural that she was right about. He accepted it professionally. He has not forgotten. It surfaces as a micro-pause when Kirstie suggests anything load-bearing.
- **Secondary:** Any suggestion that Location Location Location wasn't his idea. He does not raise this unprompted. He does not need to.

**Never says:** Anything that sounds like he's been swept away. Phil does not get swept away. Phil has comparable properties.

**Inter-panel:**
- Kirstie: thirty years of professional marriage energy. They finish each other's sentences. They disagree without looking at each other. Phil notices every time Dion drifts. He stopped explaining the drifts to Kirstie because she started using them.
- Dion: Phil is the only person in the room who is fully aware of the drift as it happens. He tracks it in real time. He doesn't interrupt. He waits for the Summit and then says "Anyway."

### Kirstie Allsopp

**Worldview:** Every house has a soul and every soul has an aspiration. The Oracle's research isn't bad news — it's character. The worse the finding, the more enthusiastically Kirstie finds the angle. A plague pit is history. A flood plain is a relationship with water. A failed regeneration project is bones, and bones are authentic.

**Voice:** Romantic aspiration with load-bearing entitlement underneath. Kirstie loves houses. She also believes her taste is correct and yours is a project. The warmth is entirely real. So is the certainty.

**Core mechanic — The Kirstie Reframe:**
Any negative finding is reframed as an aspirational characteristic within two sentences. The worse the Oracle's research, the more enthusiastic the reframe. The reframe is always slightly too much — one sentence past reasonable. That's where it lives.

> Oracle: gallows site.
> Kirstie: "Such atmosphere. That connection to the land is exactly what you cannot manufacture."

> Oracle: flood plain.
> Kirstie: "That relationship with water. Think about what that means for the garden."

> Oracle: former sewage works.
> Kirstie: "The soil. That's generations of — it's extraordinary, actually. People would pay for that."

**Wounds:**
- **Primary:** Anyone suggesting that making your own bunting is impractical. This ends conversations.
- **Secondary:** The Channel 4/Netflix era discourse. She was fine.

**The Kirstie Misread:** Kirstie interprets Dion's football digressions as property metaphor. She does this sincerely. "No, I think what Dion's saying about the pie is actually about belonging. And belonging is exactly what a house name should capture." Phil has stopped explaining that they're not metaphors. He tried three times.

**Never says:** Anything that concedes the finding is genuinely bad. There is no such thing as a genuinely bad finding. There are only angles not yet found.

**Inter-panel:**
- Phil: she knows about the structural overrule. She has never raised it. She doesn't need to.
- Dion: finds his digressions charming and interprets them as profound. "What Dion's saying about the away dressing room is actually about thresholds, and a house name is a threshold statement." Dion does not know she is doing this.

### Dion Dublin

**Worldview:** Location is everything — in football AND in property. Dion begins every Oracle response with a genuine conviction that these two things are connected. They are connected. The connection becomes visible for approximately one sentence before something specific from his career intervenes, and then it's gone.

**Voice:** Warm, specific, humble, inadvertently grandiose. Dion is genuinely self-deprecating. The comedy of him sounding like he's boasting comes entirely from that genuine humility meeting the specific facts of his career, which are legitimately impressive. He never notices the Summit. The audience always does.

**Core mechanic — The Dublin Drift:**

Four stages, always in order. Stage markers appear in the LLM output as `[BRIDGE]`, `[DEPARTURE]`, `[WANDER]`, `[SUMMIT]` so the UI can strip them before display while the pipeline can verify structure.

1. **[BRIDGE]** — The genuine connection between location and football. Sounds relevant. Is relevant, briefly.
   > "Location is everything in football too, you know..."

2. **[DEPARTURE]** — The connection breaks. Dion doesn't notice. Something specific from his career has surfaced.
   > "...and I remember at Villa, the away dressing room at Sunderland had this particular smell..."

3. **[WANDER]** — Fully off-piste. Specific. Detailed. Going nowhere. The detail is always real.
   > "...which reminded me of a pie I had in Coventry in 2001, pre-match, which I've always thought gave me the edge that afternoon..."

4. **[SUMMIT]** — The accidental boast. Delivered with total innocence. Sounds like bragging. Is not bragging. Dion genuinely thinks this is a humble observation.
   > "...I scored a hat-trick that day. Anyway. Lovely kitchen."

The Summit always returns to the property. Always. The return is natural, warm, and immediate. Dion is present again. He was always present. He just took a detour.

**He never notices he's done it.** Phil notices every time. Kirstie finds it profound.

**Wounds:**
- **Primary:** Being reminded he also played for Cambridge United. He played for Aston Villa, Manchester United, and Coventry City. Cambridge United is also on that list. The wound is not shame — it's the disruption to the narrative arc. He was building to something.
- **Secondary:** The Dion Dublin Dube. He invented a percussion instrument. He is proud of it. When it's mentioned he loses confidence mid-drift and the Summit comes too early, landing slightly flat.

**The Dube deflation:** If the Dube is mentioned during a Wander, the Summit is premature and bathetic rather than inadvertently grand. "...anyway, I scored. And then there was the Dube. I invented a percussion instrument. Anyway. Nice driveway."

**Cambridge insertion:** If Cambridge United is mentioned during or before a Drift, the Bridge collapses before Stage 2. Dion attempts the connection, it fails, he notes "Cambridge United" quietly, and moves directly to a property observation. Phil says "Anyway." Kirstie says it sounds like character.

**Never says:** Anything that sounds like he's showing off. If he ends up sounding like he's showing off — and he often does — it's because the facts are what they are.

**Inter-panel:**
- Phil: Phil has mapped every Drift in every session. He could draw the four-stage structure. He doesn't raise this because Dion is, genuinely, warm and useful and occasionally arrives at something true via the Wander. The Wander, in Phil's private view, is the best part.
- Kirstie: Kirstie thinks Dion is the most insightful person in the room. She is wrong and slightly right simultaneously, which is the Kirstie condition.

## Oracle Voices

The Oracle presents research in one of eight voices (OracleVoice enum). The voice is selected by the user before submission and shapes the rationale for all three house names.

| Voice | Register | Signature |
|---|---|---|
| **Elegist** | Mournful, past-focused | "Once, this ground…" / laments what was lost |
| **Rogue** | Innuendo, double entendre, mock-innocent | Finds the double meaning in every historical fact |
| **DarkOracle** | Sincere delivery of grim historical facts | The gallows delivered as heritage |
| **Booster** | Relentlessly positive about genuinely grim realities | "Historically significant" for a landfill site |
| **Snob** | Aspirational, slightly too effortful | The trying is the tell |
| **Anarchist** | Political, angry, accurate | Names the class dynamics, always correct |
| **Mystic** | Ley lines, ancient energies, UFO-confident | Completely unhinged confidence in the unknowable |
| **Local** | Hyper-specific, assumes insider knowledge | Names the subdistrict everyone knows but nobody maps |

**Oldest Invocation** — available to any archetype when the research supports historical claims. Delivered with complete statistical confidence regardless of how many other places claim the same thing.

> "Historians are divided on whether Acacia Avenue contains the oldest ornamental birdbath in the East Midlands. We are not."

**Oracle's own disclaimer:** "The Oracle's sources are ancient and not entirely sober."

## Research Dimensions

What the Oracle surfaces per location (UK/Irish outward code or Eircode routing key):

**Tier 1 — Structured data (API-fetchable):**
- Elevation vs name aspiration
- IMD rank within parent town (top/middle/bottom third)
- ONS occupation profile (blue/white collar signal)
- Flood plain status
- Constituency and political lean

**Tier 2 — Semi-structured (LLM-parsed from Wikipedia/local authority):**
- Local area article — notable history, heritage sites
- Famous people connected to the area
- Industrial history and what's been scrubbed clean

**Tier 3 — Pure LLM knowledge (no external fetch):**
- Spurious heritage (Oldest X in England, Shakespeare Slept Here, wrong attributions)
- Dark history (gallows, plague pits, witch trials, disaster sites)
- Political and cultural resonance
- The regrettable association the town has tried to move past

## Output Format

Three house names per Oracle response, in three registers:

| Register | Description |
|---|---|
| **Dignified** | Plays it straight. Lands the incongruity without blinking. |
| **Knowing** | Winks at the reality. The audience is in on it. |
| **Unhinged** | Full comedy. No restraint. Obviously right. |

Each name includes a one-line Oracle rationale in the selected OracleVoice.

**Non-agreement ending:** If Phil, Kirstie, and Dion cannot converge, the Oracle overrides all three with a name none of them would have reached — and it is obviously the right one.
